## 1. Why Selling is Hard — The Psychology

Selling is the hardest part of investing. Fundamental analysis tells you **what to buy and at what valuation**. Technical analysis teaches you **when to buy and when to exit**.

> "You are, face it, a bunch of emotions, prejudices and twitches, and this is all very well as long as you know it... If you don't know who you are, the stock market is an expensive place to find out." — Adam Smith

Key psychological traps:
- **Anchoring** to purchase price rather than current fundamentals
- **Loss aversion** — holding losers too long hoping they bounce back
- **Disposition effect** — selling winners too early, holding losers too long
- **Forced long-term investing** — staying in a deteriorating stock under the illusion of being a "long-term investor"
- **Errors of Omission pain** — fear that if you sell, the stock will keep going up

## 2. Investing Styles & What Drives Stock Prices

### The Four Investor Types

| Investor Type | What They Focus On |
|---|---|
| Deep Value Investor | Buying deeply undervalued businesses, often distressed |
| Value Investor | Buying quality businesses at reasonable prices |
| Momentum / Trader | Price and volume signals, short-to-medium holding periods |
| Business Momentum Investor | Earnings acceleration, fundamental catalysts playing out |

Your investor type determines **when it is correct for you to sell**.

### What Actually Makes Stocks Move — Peter Lynch's Core Rule

> "If you can follow only one bit of data, follow the earnings... sooner or later earnings make or break an investment in equities." — Peter Lynch

Stocks move for **two reasons only**:
1. **Earnings are surprising expectations** (beating or missing analyst estimates)
2. **Anticipation of earnings** (market prices in future earnings before results)

### Peter Lynch's Approach to Selling
- Bought and sold **14,000+ stocks** at Magellan Fund
- Average holding period: **6 months per stock**
- Approach: entirely **Business Momentum Focused**

> "If I was doing my job correctly, the turnover rate would have been even higher." — Peter Lynch

**Lesson:** Do not confuse holding forever with being a great investor. Exit when the thesis expires.

## 3. Earnings Expectations — The Engine of Stock Prices

### Who Sets Expectations?
- Analysts build collective **consensus earnings expectations**
- Standard recommendations: Buy / Sell / Neutral / Hold

### How Analysts Build Estimates
1. Revenue Projection (from guidance, concalls, channel checks)
2. Apply Expected Margins → subtract taxes → arrive at **EPS**

### Why Estimate Revisions Are Powerful
- Estimates revised **upward by 5%+** → stocks tend to outperform
- Estimates revised **downward by 5%+** → stocks tend to underperform

Watch analyst estimate revisions as an early warning system. Downward revisions = sell signal.

### There Are No Permanent Growth Companies
> There are always only **purple patches of compounding** — periods of exceptional growth that eventually mature.

Examples: Steve Ballmer era at Microsoft, Agrochemical consolidation, Laurus Labs 2009 inflection.

## 4. The Business Lifecycle & Purple Patch Concept

### Earnings Expectation Curve

```
Earnings
   |              *** PEAK PURPLE PATCH ***
   |           ***                        ***
   |        ***                              ***
   |     ***   <- Entry Point                  ***
   |  ***                                         *** <- Exit Zone
   |_______________________________________________ Time
     Neglect      Discovery    Acceleration    Maturation/Decline
```

- **Neglect phase**: Fundamentals building, cheap valuations — ideal buy zone
- **Discovery phase**: Earnings beats begin, institutions enter
- **Acceleration phase**: Strong compounding, momentum investors enter
- **Maturation/Decline phase**: Earnings growth slows — exit zone

## 5. Stage Analysis (Stan Weinstein Framework)

The **30-week moving average (30WMA)** is the central indicator throughout all stages.

### Stage 1: Basing Stage
- Horizontal price movement (sideways)
- Price oscillates above and below the 30WMA
- Ownership transfers from weak hands to strong hands
- **Action:** Watch; begin accumulating carefully. Wait for Stage 2 breakout before full commitment.

### Stage 2: Advancing Stage
- Aggressive institutional accumulation
- High price + volume action; strong breakout from base
- Stock holds above the rising 30WMA
- **Action (Investor):** Buy at Stage 2 start or on pullback to 30WMA.

### Stage 3: Consolidation / Topping Stage
- Stock trends sideways, losing upward momentum
- 30WMA flattens and begins to curl down
- Forms top patterns (head-and-shoulders, double top)
- **Action:** Begin reducing; sell proactively before Stage 4 breakdown.

### Stage 4: Breakdown / Downtrend Stage
- Stock breaks below 200 DMA/EMA on high volume
- Sustained fall; sellers control price action
- **Rule (Weinstein): NEVER buy in Stage 4. Never average down into Stage 4.**

### Stage Analysis Summary

| Stage | Name | 30WMA Direction | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Basing | Flat | Watch, accumulate carefully |
| 2 | Advancing | Rising | **BUY** |
| 3 | Topping | Flat/Curling Down | **SELL proactively** |
| 4 | Breakdown | Falling | **DO NOT BUY — exit all** |

## 6. Technical Analysis Foundations for Selling

> "The ultimate fundamental in any auction marketplace is the law of demand and supply. Technical Analysis is the study of demand and supply."

- **Price** = Trend former
- **Volume** = Weight of evidence

> "Volume is a gauge on how powerful the buyers are." — Stan Weinstein

### Three Types of Trends

| Trend | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Uptrend | Higher Highs, Higher Lows |
| Sideways | Horizontal movement; supply and demand equal |
| Downtrend | Lower Highs, Lower Lows |

**Sell signal:** When an uptrend breaks — stock stops making Higher Highs and Higher Lows.

### Price/Volume Combinations

**Bullish (favour holding):**
- Price up + Volume up → Strong demand, trend healthy
- Price pulls back + Volume low → Weak selling, likely to recover

**Bearish (favour selling):**
- Price down + Volume up → Strong supply, distribution occurring
- Price up + Volume low → Weak rally, low conviction

### Timeframes
- **Investors:** Weekly charts primary; monthly for confirmation
- **Traders:** Daily charts

## 7. Flow of Information in Markets

**Information cascade — who knows first:**

```
1. INSIDERS & CLOSE PEOPLE      ← Know first (material non-public information)
        ↓
2. INSTITUTIONS                 ← Large funds pick up signals via channel checks
        ↓
3. NEWS MEDIA                   ← Story hits Bloomberg, CNBC, press
        ↓
4. RESULTS / FILINGS            ← Confirmed in quarterly numbers
        ↓
5. RETAIL INVESTORS             ← Often the last to know
```

**Implication for selling:**
- By the time a bullish story hits mainstream news, institutions may already be selling
- High volume at a topping stock = institutions distributing to retail buyers
- Do not hold because "the thesis is intact" if price/volume action signals Stage 3/4

## 8. Decision Making — 12 Maxims of Probabilistic Thinking

Source: SOIC Decision Making webinar, drawing on frameworks by Richard Zeckhauser.

### Maxim 1: When Confused, Go to an Extreme Case
Push a decision to its logical extreme to see the underlying structure.
**Investor application:** Best-case / worst-case analysis to size positions and set sell thresholds.

### Maxim 2: When Confused, Go to a Simple Case
**Rule of Multiplication:**
- 90% chance an undesirable event does NOT happen in a year
- 4 such independent events: Chance NONE occur = 0.90⁴ = **65%**
- Chance AT LEAST ONE occurs = **35%**
**Investor application:** Multiple small risks compound into large aggregate portfolio risk.

### Maxim 3: Don't Take Refuge in Complexity
The best investment theses can be stated simply. If you cannot explain why you own a stock in 2-3 sentences, you don't understand when to sell it.

### Maxim 4: Use Everyday Analogues for Complex Situations
Stage Analysis maps to an airplane: takeoff (Stage 2), cruising (early Stage 3), descent (late Stage 3), crash landing (Stage 4).

### Maxim 5: The World is Much More Uncertain Than You Think
> "Certainty, and near certainty, is an illusion. Over the course of our lives we will experience several low-frequency and high-severity events. Think of COVID-19 or 9/11."
**Investor application:** Build portfolios to survive extreme scenarios. A sell discipline matters precisely because the future is uncertain.

### Maxim 6: Think Probabilistically
Every outcome exists on a probability distribution, not a single predicted path.
**Investor application:** When deciding to sell — what is the probability the thesis has played out vs. the probability it has more to run?

### Maxim 7: Uncertainty is the Friend of the Status Quo
> **DO NOT BE A FORCED LONG-TERM INVESTOR.**

People disproportionately do nothing even when an accessible alternative would be superior.
**Investor application:** Review each holding on current merits, not on your original buy decision. Years of holding are irrelevant to whether to hold for one more year.

### Maxim 8: Good Decisions Sometimes Have Poor Outcomes
Outcome quality ≠ Decision quality.
**Investor application:** Judge decisions by process, not outcome. Keep a decision journal — write WHY you bought and WHY you sold.

### Maxim 9: Some Decisions Have a High Probability of a Bad Outcome
- Stocks in Stage 4 carry structurally high probability of further decline
- Chasing 10x-run hype stocks without earnings support = high-probability-of-loss decision
- Avoid these even when they "feel" right

### Maxim 10: Errors of Commission = Errors of Omission

**Error of Commission:** Bought at ₹100. Sold at ₹200. Stock went to ₹500.
**Error of Omission:** Studied stock at ₹200. Didn't buy. Stock went to ₹600.

Errors of omission are often more painful — and less tracked.
**Investor application:** Track both: stocks sold too early AND stocks never bought but should have been.

### Maxim 11: Don't Be Limited by Options in Front of You
**Opportunity cost is a critical mental model.**
**Investor application:** Before holding a mediocre position, ask: "Is there a better use of this capital right now?" Study as many sectors as possible.

### Maxim 12: Information is Only Valuable If It Can Change Your Decision
Gathering more information feels productive but is often noise.
**Investor application:** Define in advance what information would make you sell — then act when it arrives. If a Stage 4 breakdown won't make you sell, you have hope, not a sell discipline.

## 9. Sell Decision Checklist & Framework Summary

### Fundamental Sell Triggers
- [ ] Thesis has fully played out — no new catalyst remains
- [ ] Earnings growth decelerating materially (not a one-quarter blip — a change in trajectory)
- [ ] Analyst estimates revised downward by 5%+ (especially multiple analysts simultaneously)
- [ ] Management guidance is being cut
- [ ] Competitive moat eroding — market share loss, pricing pressure, disruptive competitor
- [ ] Business has entered the Maturation/Decline phase of the Earnings Expectation Curve
- [ ] Purple patch has clearly ended — sector or company moved from acceleration to stagnation

### Technical Sell Triggers (Stage Analysis)
- [ ] Stock has entered Stage 3 — 30WMA flattened, price oscillating widely → begin reducing
- [ ] Stage 4 breakdown confirmed — stock breaks below 200 DMA/EMA on high volume → exit
- [ ] Lower High formed after a Stage 2 run — uptrend structure breaking
- [ ] High-volume distribution at the top — price flat/rising on very high volume = institutions selling
- [ ] Stock below 30WMA and 30WMA now declining — structural downtrend established

### Portfolio / Decision Quality Triggers
- [ ] Opportunity cost check: Is there a demonstrably better use for this capital?
- [ ] Status quo check: Am I holding from inertia or from intact thesis?
- [ ] Probability check: If I didn't own this today, would I buy it at this price? If No — sell.
- [ ] Forced long-term investor test: Am I using "long-term investor" label to avoid making a decision?
- [ ] Thesis reversal check: Has the specific event that would make me sell already occurred?

### What NOT to Use as a Sell Reason
- Your purchase price (anchoring bias — irrelevant to current decision)
- The fact that the stock is up a lot (profits should run if thesis holds)
- The fact that the stock is down a lot ("it can't go lower" is not analysis)
- News headlines (by the time it's news, the move is often complete)
- Analyst target prices as precise sell triggers (they are directional, not precise)

### Decision Quality Principles Summary

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| Go to extreme cases | Use best/worst case to clarify sell decisions under uncertainty |
| Think probabilistically | Assign probabilities; sell when expected value turns negative |
| Avoid status quo bias | Review every position on current merit, not original purchase basis |
| Track errors of omission | Count missed sells AND missed buys — both are real errors |
| Check opportunity costs | Always compare current holding to best available alternative |
| Separate decision from outcome | Good sell process can produce bad outcome — that's acceptable |
| Act when information changes your view | Define sell triggers in advance; act when they're hit |
