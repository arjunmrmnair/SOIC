# 1. BALANCE SHEET

## 1.1 What is a Balance Sheet?

- A balance sheet displays a company's **total assets** and **how its assets are financed**, either through debt or equity.
- It gives an idea of the **financial health** of an organisation.
- It is like a **snapshot** of the **financial position** at a **specified time**.
  - Example: Laurus Labs Balance Sheet is a snapshot of its financial position as of 31st March 2022.

**When is it released?**
- Balance Sheets are released with **H1 and H2 results**.
- H1 = Balance sheet up till **30th September** (Unaudited)
- H2 = Balance sheet up till **31st March** (Audited)

---

## 1.2 The Balance Sheet Formula

```
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholder's Equity
```
OR equivalently:
```
Assets - Liabilities = Shareholder's Equity
```

- **Assets** = Cash, inventory, property
- **Liabilities** = Rent, wages, utilities, taxes, loans
- **Shareholder's Equity** = Retained earnings

**Key principle:** Sources of funds (Liabilities + Equity) will ALWAYS equal application of funds (Assets). The balance sheet always balances.

---

## 1.3 Accrual Principle of Accounting

Most companies use the **accrual basis accounting method**:

- Revenue is **recognised when it is earned**, rather than when it is received.
- This causes a **disconnect between net income and actual cash flow** because not all transactions in net income on the income statement involve actual cash items.
- Therefore, certain items must be **re-evaluated** when calculating cash flow from operations.
- **Cash Flow is concerned about the actual cash** that flows into the company and the cash that flows out.

**History note:** In 1494, the first book on double-entry accounting was published by **Luca Pacioli**, an Italian friar. His impact was so great that five centuries later, accountants from around the world gathered in the Italian village of San Sepulcro to celebrate the anniversary of the book's publication.

---

## 1.4 Why Does a Balance Sheet Always Balance?

1. If we **add more liabilities or equity**, there would be a **corresponding increase in assets**.
2. A Balance Sheet **always Balances**. The sources of funds (Liabilities & Equity) will always be equal to the application of funds (Assets).

### Siddhant's Samosa Shop Example (Balance Sheet Mechanics)

**Day 1 Setup:**
- Siddhant starts a Samosa shop outside India Gate
- Source of funds: Own Money (INR 200) + Interest-free loan from SOIC (INR 800) = INR 1,000
- Application of funds: Buys Kitchen Equipment (INR 250) + Buys Raw Material/Inventory (INR 250) = Cash at hand INR 500

**Day 1 Balance Sheet:**

| ASSETS | | LIABILITIES | |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inventory | INR 250 | Own Equity | INR 200 |
| Fixed Assets | INR 250 | Borrowed from SOIC | INR 800 |
| Cash at hand | INR 500 | | |
| **TOTAL ASSETS** | **INR 1,000** | **TOTAL LIABILITIES** | **INR 1,000** |

**Day 1 Operations:**
- Sells 100 Samosas at Rs. 5 each, cost Rs. 1 each → Profit per Samosa = INR 4
- Income Statement: Sales = INR 500, Cost of Sales = INR 100, **Profit = INR 400**

**Introducing Credit ("Eat Now Pay Later"):**
- Out of 100 Samosas sold: 50 for cash, 50 on credit
- Cash Sales = INR 250, Cost = INR 100 → **Cash PAT = INR 150**
- Receivables = INR 250 (credit sales not yet collected)

**Day 2 Balance Sheet (after credit sales):**

| ASSETS | | LIABILITIES | |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inventory | INR 150 | Reserves (Profit) | INR 400 |
| Fixed Assets | INR 250 | Own Equity | INR 200 |
| Cash at hand | INR 750 | Borrowed from SOIC | INR 800 |
| Receivables | INR 250 | | |
| **TOTAL ASSETS** | **INR 1,400** | **TOTAL LIABILITIES** | **INR 1,400** |

**Key question:** What if the picnic-goers do NOT repay Siddhant? This is exactly why a Cash Flow Statement is extremely important.

## 1.5 Equity

**What is Equity?**
- Equity = Assets - Liabilities
- Also known as **Net Worth** of the company or **Book Value** of the company
- Represents the amount of money that would be **returned to shareholders** if all assets were liquidated and all debt paid off

**Two components of Equity:**
1. **Equity Share Capital**
2. **Other Equity** (Reserves and Surplus)

### 1.5.1 Equity Share Capital

- **Share Capital = Face Value × Number of Shares Outstanding**
- Share capital of a company DOES NOT CHANGE based on market price — it is always calculated on the basis of Face Value (FV).

**Example — Bharat Rasayan:**
- 42,48,740 equity shares of Rs. 10 each
- Share capital = Rs. 4.25 crores (42.48 Lakhs × Rs. 10)

**Example — Burger company:**
- Investment of Rs. 1 Lakh, 10,000 shares at FV Rs. 10 each → Share capital = Rs. 1 lakh

### 1.5.2 Other Equity (Reserves and Surplus)

Components include:
- **Retained Earnings** — accumulated profits of the company since inception; net profit/loss from P&L transferred here
- **General Reserves** — created by the company for safety purposes (contingencies like fire or other losses)
- **Capital Reserve** — from capital gains
- **Capital Redemption Reserve**
- **Securities Premium** — the premium above face value collected during share issuance

**Securities Premium Example:**
- After 5 years, company launches IPO at Rs. 500 (FV = Rs. 10)
- Face value goes to Share Capital, the Rs. 490 premium goes to **Securities Premium** (part of Other Equity)
- 1 lakh shares × Rs. 10 = Rs. 10 lakhs in Share Capital; 1 lakh shares × Rs. 490 = Rs. 490 lakhs in Securities Premium

**Can Retained Earnings turn negative?**
- YES — if the company keeps reporting losses and the amount of losses exceeds retained earnings.
- Example: **SpiceJet** — retained earnings turned negative due to persistent losses.

### 1.5.3 Ways to Raise Equity

1. **Primary Market Issue (IPO)** — initial public offering to general public
2. **Warrants** — equity infused by promoters
3. **Rights Issue** — invitation to existing shareholders to purchase additional shares at a discounted price within a specific period; unlike IPO, not offered to general public; promoters can choose to participate or not
4. **QIP (Qualified Institutional Placement)** — when money is raised from domestic institutional investors

**Example — Saregama QIP:**
- Raised Rs. 74,999.97 Lakhs (~Rs. 750 crores) by allotting 18,50,937 equity shares at FV Rs. 10 each
- Utilisation: Issue expenses (Rs. 1,684.34 L) + Content acquisition (Rs. 2,267.50 L) + Balance in mutual funds/FDs (Rs. 71,048.13 L)

**Is Equity a part of Liabilities?**
- YES — Equity is always written with liabilities. An organisation borrows from its owners and outsiders. The only difference is outsiders demand repayment within a fixed time.

---

## 1.6 Types of Liabilities

**Two Types:**
1. **Non-Current Liabilities** — due for more than 1 year
2. **Current Liabilities** — due for less than 1 year

### 1.6.1 Non-Current Liabilities (4 types):

1. **Long-Term Borrowings (LTB)** — maturity of 12 months or longer
   - Can range from 12 months to 30+ years
   - Types: Bank loans (secured/unsecured), Debentures, Bonds, Loans from Related Parties, Finance Lease Obligations, Deposits
2. **Long-Term Provisions**
3. **Other Long-Term Liabilities**
4. **Deferred Tax Liabilities**

**Key note:** The current proportion of long-term debt that needs to be repaid within 1 year is reclassified to **Current Liabilities**.

### 1.6.2 Current Liabilities

- Trade Payables
- Short-term borrowings
- Current maturities of long-term debt
- Provisions
- Other current liabilities

---

## 1.7 Assets

**Two Types:**
1. **Non-Current Assets** — held for more than 1 year
2. **Current Assets** — can be converted to cash within 1 year

### 1.7.1 Non-Current Assets include:
- Property, Plant and Equipment (PPE) / Fixed Assets
- Capital Work in Progress (CWIP)
- Right of Use Assets
- Intangible Assets
- Investments
- Deferred Tax Assets

### 1.7.2 Current Assets include:
- Inventories
- Trade Receivables
- Cash and Cash Equivalents
- Other Bank Balances
- Loans
- Other Financial Assets

---

## 1.8 Key Balance Sheet Ratios

- **Debt/Equity Ratio** = Total Debt / Equity
- **Current Ratio** = Current Assets / Current Liabilities (healthy: >1)
- **Book Value per Share** = Total Equity / Number of Shares Outstanding

# 2. INCOME STATEMENT (P&L)

## 2.1 What is an Income Statement?

Also known as the **profit and loss statement (P&L)**, or the **statement of revenue and expense**, the income statement primarily focuses on the **company's revenue and expenses during a particular period**.

```
Net Profit = Total Revenue - Total Expenses
```

- **Top-line** = Sales/Revenue (what a company earns at the top)
- **Bottom-line** = PAT/Net Profit (what remains after all deductions)
- **EBITDA** = Cash Operating Profits

**Case studies covered:** L&T, Tata Power, V3 Beverages, Saregama, Narayana Hrudayalaya (NH), ITC

---

## 2.2 The Complete P&L Structure (Simplified Income Statement)

```
SALES (Revenue from Core Ops + Non Core)
- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
= GROSS PROFIT
- Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A)
= EBITDA (= EBIT = Operating Profit = PBIT)
- Depreciation & Amortisation
= EBIT
- Interest / Finance Cost
= PROFIT BEFORE TAX (PBT)
- Taxes
= PROFIT AFTER TAX (PAT)

EPS = PAT / Number of Shares Outstanding
```

---

1. Revenue from **core** operations
2. Revenue from **non-core** operations / Other Operating Revenue

**Sales = Volume × Realisations (Value/Price)**

## 2.4 Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

**Definition:** Direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold. It is the **raw material cost** that the company requires to manufacture finished goods.

**Formula:**
```
COGS = Opening Stock + Purchases - Closing Stock
```

**Note:** Closing stock of the previous year will always be the opening stock of the current year.

### 2.4.1 Purchases of Stock in Trade

Goods that are **bought and sold without any processing**. Refers to all purchases of finished goods that the company buys towards conducting its business.

### 2.4.2 Change in Inventory of Finished Goods

- Refers to costs incurred in the past on goods sold in the current year.
- **Negative amount** = company produced more items than it sold (or inventory gains occurred).
- **Positive amount** = company might have incurred an inventory loss.

**Example — Jubilant Ingrevia (Acetic Acid):**
- Q1FY22: Inventory GAINS — change in inventory was negative (-4063 lakhs) indicating inventory buildup
- Q4FY22: Inventory LOSSES — change in inventory was positive (1706 lakhs)

**Example — Ambika Cotton:**
- Q1FY21: Change in inventories = -893 (inventory gain, built more than sold)
- Q4FY21: Change = +790 (inventory loss, sold from prior stock)

**Granules India — Raw Material (PAP) Supplier Power:**
- Granules was earlier dependent on China for 27% of its PAP (Para-Aminophenol) raw material for Paracetamol
- Structural change: Now sourcing from Indian suppliers → reduces input cost by ~13%
- This supplier shift directly improves gross margins

## 2.5 The 5 Margin Levels — Calculated for Siddhant's Samosa Shop

Using: Sales = INR 50, COGS = INR 10, SG&A = INR 10, Depreciation = INR 2, Interest = INR 0, Tax = INR 8

### 1. Gross Margin
```
Gross Profit = Sales - COGS = 50 - 10 = INR 40
Gross Margin % = (40/50) × 100 = 80%
```

### 2. EBITDA Margin
```
EBITDA = Gross Profit - SG&A = 40 - 10 = INR 30
EBITDA Margin % = (30/50) × 100 = 60%
```
EBITDA = also called "Cash Operating Profits"

### 3. EBIT Margin (Operating Margin)
```
EBIT = EBITDA - Depreciation = 30 - 2 = INR 28
EBIT Margin % = (28/50) × 100 = 56%
```

### 4. Profit Before Tax (PBT)
```
PBT = EBIT - Interest = 28 - 0 = INR 28
```

### 5. Profit After Tax (PAT) / Net Profit Margin
```
PAT = PBT - Tax = 28 - 8 = INR 20
PAT Margin % = (20/50) × 100 = 40%
```

### 6. Earnings Per Share (EPS)
```
EPS = PAT / Number of Outstanding Shares = 20 / 20 = Rs. 1
```

# 3. CASH FLOW STATEMENT

## 3.1 What is a Cash Flow Statement?

The cash flow statement (**CFS**) is a financial statement that **summarises the movement of cash and cash equivalents (CCE)** that come in and go out of a company.

- The CFS **measures how well a company manages its cash position**, meaning how well the company generates cash to pay its debt obligations and fund its operating expenses.
- As one of the three main financial statements, the **CFS complements the balance sheet and the income statement**.

---

## 3.2 Why is Cash Flow Important?

Most companies use the **accrual basis accounting method**:
- Revenue is recognised when earned, not when received.
- This causes a **disconnect between net income and actual cash flow**.
- Cash Flow is concerned about **actual cash** flowing in and out.

**The critical question:** What if receivables are never collected? A company can show profits on paper but have no cash. This is why the CFS is extremely important.

---

## 3.3 Three Sections of a Cash Flow Statement

```
1. Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)    — core business activities
2. Cash Flow from Investing (CFI)     — investing activities
3. Cash Flow from Financing (CFA)     — debt repayment, equity raising
```

---

## 3.4 Cash Flow from Operations (CFO) — Indirect Method

**Starting point:** Net Profit / (Loss) before tax from P&L

**Adjustments made (4 types):**

### Step 1 — Add back Depreciation
- Depreciation = cost of physical assets divided over the years of use
- **It is a NON-CASH expense** — the actual cash outflow happened when the asset was purchased (shows in CFI)
- Hence, depreciation is **added back** to arrive at cash flow
- Example: Asset worth Rs. 1 lakh used over 5 years → Depreciation = Rs. 20,000/year (non-cash accounting entry)

### Step 2 — Add back Finance Cost (Interest)
- Finance cost = interest paid on borrowings
- Since interest is **not part of day-to-day business activities**, it is added back in CFO and shown separately in CFA
- Finance cost is removed from operating activities and placed in financing activities

### Step 3 — Adjust Non-Core Operating Income
- **Dividend received** is subtracted (goes to CFI)
- **Loss on Sale of assets** is added back (non-cash for operations)
- **Gain on Sale of assets** is subtracted (goes to CFI)
- Reason: These are NOT part of core business activities

### Step 4 — Changes in Working Capital
After all adjustments → arrive at **Operating Profit before Working Capital Changes**
Then adjust for changes in working capital:
- Changes in Trade Receivables
- Changes in Inventories
- Changes in Trade Payables

**Golden Trick:**
- Current Assets increase → Cash Flow DECREASES (inversely proportional)
- Current Assets decrease → Cash Flow INCREASES
- Current Liabilities increase → Cash Flow INCREASES (directly proportional)
- Current Liabilities decrease → Cash Flow DECREASES

**Formula:** CFO = EBITDA ± Working Capital Changes - Taxes Paid

---

## 3.5 Working Capital and Cash Conversion Cycle

**Working Capital** = capital of a business used in its day-to-day trading operations.

**Key components:**
- Trade Receivables
- Inventory
- Trade Payables

**Cash Conversion Cycle:**
- **Higher Trade Receivables** → lesser cash flow (cash blocked in debtors)
- **Lower Inventory** → lesser cash blocked → higher cash flow
- **Higher Payables** → you are using someone else's money → higher cash flow (and vice versa)

---

## 3.6 GNFC Case Study — Exceptional Item in CFO

**GNFC (Gujarat Narmada Valley Fertilizers & Chemicals), FY2015:**
- TDI-II plant at Dahej failed to stabilize
- Loss from TDI-II operations = Rs. 440 Crore
- Provision for impairment of TDI-II assets = **Rs. 330 Crore** (exceptional item written off)

**Question:** Does a Rs. 330 Crore asset write-off appear in Cash Flow from Operations?
**Answer: YES — it gets ADDED BACK** to CFO (it is a non-cash exceptional item, like a large depreciation)

From GNFC Cash Flow FY2015:
- Net Profit before Tax = (45,206.81) lakhs [a loss]
- Depreciation added back = 26,651.74 lakhs
- Provision for Impairment of Fixed Assets added back = 33,000.00 lakhs
- Net Cash Flow from Operating Activities = **56,508.44 lakhs** (positive despite net loss!)

This shows how a company can have negative profits but positive operating cash flows due to large non-cash charges.

## 3.7 B2G vs. B2C Working Capital

**Which business has higher working capital requirements?**
- **B2G (Business to Government)** — governments are slow payers; very long receivable days → HIGH working capital requirement
- **B2C (Business to Consumer)** — cash/instant payment → LOW working capital requirement

## 3.9 Cash Flow from Investing (CFI)

- Purchase of Fixed Assets / Capex (negative cash flow — cash going out)
- Sale of Fixed Assets (positive cash flow)
- Purchase of Investments
- Sale of Investments
- Interest received (from FDs, ICDs)
- Dividend received

---

## 3.10 Cash Flow from Financing (CFA)

Cash that flows into or out of the company through financing activities:
- Repayment of debt (negative)
- Raising of debt (positive)
- Payment of interest (negative)
- Dividend paid (negative)
- Proceeds from share issuance (positive)

# 4. ROCE vs ROIC vs ROE

## 4.1 Return on Equity (ROE)

### What is ROE?

- ROE indicates a company's **profitability** by measuring how much the **shareholders earned** for their investment in the company.
- ROE signifies how good the company is in **generating returns** on the investment received from shareholders.

**Formula:**
```
ROE = Net Profit (PAT) / Average Equity (Net Worth)

Where Net Worth = Equity Share Capital + Reserves and Surplus
```

**DuPont Formula for ROE:**
```
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Financial Leverage
    = (Net Profit/Sales) × (Sales/Assets) × (Assets/Equity)
```

**Basic example:**
- Siddhant: Own equity = INR 100, Borrowings = INR 100, Net Profit = INR 50
- ROE = 50/100 = **50%**
- ROCE = 50/200 = **25%**

---

## 4.2 Why the Market Rallied When Corporate Tax Cut Was Announced (2019)

- Sensex rallied 1,922 points (5.3%) to 38,015
- Nifty surged 569 points (5.3%) to 11,274.2
- **Reason:** Lower taxes → Higher PAT → Higher ROE → Companies more valuable
- ROE = PAT / Equity. When tax rate drops, PAT rises, so ROE rises → market re-rates upward.

---

## 4.3 Cyclicals — Don't Get Fooled by ROE

In cyclical businesses, ROE is misleading:
- At the **bottom of the cycle**: ROE will be **negative or non-existent** (no earnings). PE ratio will be very high.
- At the **peak of the cycle**: ROE will be **40–50%+**. People will think "what a great business!"
- Cyclicality is often caused by **margin cycles driven by raw material prices**

**Examples of Cyclical Raw Material Impact:**
- **Tyre companies**: RM basket rose ~50% from FY21 average → Gross margins fell over 10 percentage points (from ~42% to ~30%)
  - Natural rubber: 21% of sales, 160 bps EBITDA impact per 10% change
  - Synthetic rubber: 10% of sales, 80 bps impact
  - Carbon Black: 13% of sales, 100 bps impact

---

## 4.5 Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)

### What is ROCE?

**ROCE = EBIT / Capital Employed**

Where:
```
Capital Employed = Gross Block + Working Capital
Working Capital = Receivables + Inventory - Payables
```

### Unit Economics — 5 Things Required to Calculate ROCE:

1. **Gross Block** — investment in land, equipment, machinery and other assets
2. **Gross Asset Turns** = Revenue / Gross Block
3. **EBIT Margins** = EBIT / Sales × 100
4. **Working Capital Days** = 365 / Working Capital Turnover
5. **Capital Employed** = Gross Block + Working Capital

### ROCE Calculation Examples:

**Example 1:**
- Gross Block = 200 Cr, Sales = 400 Cr, Gross Block Turnover = 2X
- EBIT Margin = 20% → EBIT = 80 Cr
- Working Capital = 50% of sales = 200 Cr
- Capital Employed = 200 + 200 = 400 Cr
- **ROCE = 80/400 = 20%**

**Example 2:** (lower asset turns)
- Gross Block = 200 Cr, Sales = 200 Cr, Turnover = 1X
- EBIT = 20% × 200 = 40 Cr, Working Capital = 50% × 200 = 100 Cr
- Capital Employed = 200 + 100 = 300 Cr
- **ROCE = 40/300 = 13%**

**Example 3:** (same as Ex. 2 but lower working capital)
- Same as Example 2 but Working Capital = 20% × 200 = 40 Cr
- Capital Employed = 200 + 40 = 240 Cr
- **ROCE = 48/240 = 20%**

**Example 4:** (the dream business — zero working capital, high asset turns)
- Gross Block = 200 Cr, Sales = 2000 Cr, Turnover = 10X
- EBIT Margin = 50% → EBIT = 1000 Cr
- Working Capital = 0% × 2000 = 0 Cr
- Capital Employed = 200 + 0 = 200 Cr
- **ROCE = 1000/200 = 500%**

| WC Turnover | 9,254/4,557 = 2X |
| WC Days | 365/2 = 177.5 days |
| Capital Employed | 6,101 + 4,557 = 10,658 Cr |
| **ROCE** | **3,544/10,658 = 34%** |

### Neuland Labs (Lower Value-Chain Pharma)

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Gross Block | 671 Cr |
| Sales | 971 Cr |
| Gross Block Turnover | 1.5X |
| EBIT | 93 Cr (10% EBIT margin) |
| Working Capital | 387 Cr |
| WC Turnover | 2.5X, WC Days = 146 days |
| Capital Employed | 671 + 387 = 1,058 Cr |
| **ROCE** | **93/1,058 = 9%** |

**Conclusion:** Divis has higher working capital intensity but its **38% EBIT margins** allow superior unit economics. Neuland has lower WC intensity but inferior margins and asset turns → ROCE of only 9% vs Divis's 34%. Neuland operates at the lower end of the pharma value chain.

### APL Apollo — Reducing Working Capital → Higher ROCE

APL Apollo switched to **Cash & Carry Model** → dramatically reduced working capital days:

| Year | Net WC Days | ROCE |
|---|---|---|
| FY17 | 40 | 21.4% |
| FY18 | 43 | 20.8% |
| FY19 | 29 | 18.4% |
| FY20 | 25 | 18.4% |
| FY21 | 8 | 26.5% |
| FY22 | 7 | **34.5%** |

**Key insight:** Reducing WC days from 40 to 7 while growing the business → ROCE nearly doubled.

### Narayana Hrudayalaya — ROCE Expanding as Hospitals Mature

NH hospitals: As mature hospitals break even and margins start expanding → ROCE expands

| Year | OPM% | ROCE% |
|---|---|---|
| FY2018 | 10% | 9% |
| FY2019 | 10% | 9% |
| FY2020 | 14% | 14% |
| FY2021 | 7% | 2% |
| FY2022 | 18% | **25%** |

NH has **negative cash conversion cycle** (days payable > days receivable + inventory days) → receivables are low, payables are high → working capital negative → hospitals are actually funded by suppliers.

### Rushil Decor (MDF — Increasing Capacity Utilisation)

- Unit 1 (Chikmagalur, Karnataka): 90,000 CBM/annum; FY22 utilisation 76%, Q1FY23 85%
- Unit 2 (Visakhapatnam, Andhra Pradesh): 2,40,000 CBM/annum; FY22 utilisation 56%, Q1FY23 73%
- Total Value Addition: 29%
- **Risk:** India MDF industry capacity expected to expand to 2.81M CBM by FY25 → utilisation dropping to 64% → margin pressure (EBITDA margins expected to compress from 27% FY22 to pressured levels in FY24/25)

### KEI Industries — Working Capital Watch

- NWC/OI: 28% in FY2022 (improved from 33% in prior year)
- Debtor days reduced to 90 days (PY: 119) due to increasing retail sales mix
- Creditor days deliberately reduced to 61 days (PY: 97) to save finance cost (LC charges)

### Bajaj Electricals — Demerger of EPC Business

- EPC business: lower operating margin + highly working capital intensive
- Consumer products segment: higher margin, lower working capital
- Demerger of EPC into Bajel Projects Ltd (BPL) → should enhance credit profile of remaining BEL entity

## 4.7 ROCE Conclusions — Key Thresholds

| Business Type | Key Metrics | Required ROCE |
|---|---|---|
| High WC + Low Margins | Lethal combination | Unit economics never sustainable — avoid |
| High WC businesses | Need high margins to compensate | Sustainable only with strong pricing power |
| Low WC + High Asset Turns | Truly asset-light; often regulatory moats, licence-based moats, exchanges | Very high ROCE naturally |
| Moderate WC (25-35%) + Moderate Margins (15%) | Must have minimum 2x asset turns | To achieve 20%+ ROCE |
| Very low margins (6-7%) + very low WC + very high asset turns | Commodity-like businesses | Can still achieve 20%+ ROCE |

**Rule of thumb for respectable ROCE:** Target 20%+ ROCE. Compare cost of capital (typically 12-15%) — ROCE must exceed this to create shareholder value.

## 4.8 ROIC vs ROCE

**ROCE uses Gross Block + Working Capital** (i.e., total capital deployed including depreciated assets at gross value)

**ROIC** typically uses Net Capital Employed:
```
ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital
NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax = EBIT × (1 - Tax Rate)
Invested Capital = Total Equity + Total Debt - Cash
```

ROCE is more commonly used in Indian equity analysis. ROIC gives a more precise picture for companies with large cash hoards or significant goodwill from acquisitions.

## 5. CROSS-STATEMENT LINKAGES AND INVESTMENT IMPLICATIONS

### 5.1 How the Three Statements Link

1. **Income Statement → Balance Sheet:** Net profit flows into Retained Earnings (equity); depreciation reduces asset book value
2. **Income Statement → Cash Flow:** Net profit is the starting point for CFO (indirect method)
3. **Balance Sheet → Cash Flow:** Changes in working capital items (receivables, inventory, payables) appear as adjustments in CFO; capex appears in CFI; debt movements in CFA
4. **Cash Flow → Balance Sheet:** Ending cash balance on CFS = Cash on Balance Sheet

### 5.2 Quality of Earnings Check

**CFO/PAT Ratio:**
- CFO > PAT consistently → high quality earnings (Syngene example: CFO = 446-698 vs PAT = 305-412)
- CFO < PAT persistently → earnings may be accrual-inflated; receivables building up; potential red flag

**Working Capital trend:**
- Receivable days expanding rapidly without revenue growth explanation → check for channel stuffing / fake sales
- Payable days expanding beyond industry norms → suppliers being squeezed; may indicate liquidity stress

### 5.3 Red Flags to Watch

1. PAT growing but CFO declining → earnings quality deteriorating
2. Receivables growing faster than revenue → potential revenue recognition issues
3. Large one-time impairments/write-offs → check whether reflected correctly in CFO (added back) vs. the underlying cash loss
4. Negative retained earnings (Spicejet example) → sustained losses eroding equity
5. Borrowings rising rapidly while ROCE declining → destroying shareholder value
6. Working capital days expanding in a B2B business without explanation → possible channel stuffing or collection issues

### 5.4 Capacity Utilisation and Asset Turns

**Capacity Utilisation Concept:**
- If an asset can produce 100 units but is currently making 50 units → utilisation = 50%
- As utilisation rises → asset turns improve → ROCE improves WITHOUT new capex
- This operating leverage creates sharp earnings growth at higher utilisation
- Example: Rushil Decor Unit 2 went from 56% → 73% utilisation in one year → significant margin/ROCE improvement

**Narayana Hrudayalaya Hospital Example:**
- New hospitals lose money in early years (low bed utilisation, high fixed costs)
- As hospitals mature and occupancy rises → fixed costs get absorbed → margins expand sharply → ROCE jumps from 2% (FY21) to 25% (FY22)

## 6. QUICK REFERENCE — KEY FORMULAS

```
Balance Sheet:           Assets = Liabilities + Equity
Equity:                  Assets - Liabilities
Share Capital:           Face Value × Shares Outstanding
Retained Earnings:       Accumulated profits since inception

Gross Profit:            Sales - COGS
COGS:                    Opening Stock + Purchases - Closing Stock
EBITDA:                  Gross Profit - SG&A
EBIT:                    EBITDA - Depreciation
PBT:                     EBIT - Interest
PAT:                     PBT - Tax
EPS:                     PAT / Shares Outstanding

Gross Margin %:          (Gross Profit / Sales) × 100
EBITDA Margin %:         (EBITDA / Sales) × 100
EBIT Margin %:           (EBIT / Sales) × 100
PAT Margin %:            (PAT / Sales) × 100

ROE:                     PAT / Average Equity
ROE (DuPont):            (PAT/Sales) × (Sales/Assets) × (Assets/Equity)

ROCE:                    EBIT / Capital Employed
Capital Employed:        Gross Block + Working Capital
Working Capital:         Receivables + Inventory - Payables
Gross Asset Turns:       Revenue / Gross Block
WC Turnover:             Sales / Working Capital
WC Days:                 365 / WC Turnover

CFO (indirect method):   Net Profit + Depreciation + Finance Cost
                         ± Non-core income adjustments
                         ± Working Capital Changes
                         - Taxes Paid
```
