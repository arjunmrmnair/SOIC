## 1. Philosophy of Valuation

### The Blind Men and the Elephant
Valuation is not a single number — it is a multi-dimensional exercise. Each valuation framework touches a different part of the truth. The mistake investors make is claiming absolute truth from one metric alone. Use multiple lenses.

### SOIC's Investing Spectrum
Different investors operate at different points on this spectrum:
- **Deep Value Investing** — buy at large discounts to intrinsic value
- **Growth at Reasonable Prices (GARP)** — PEG < 1.5x
- **Buy Quality at Any Price** — quality compounder, ignore entry price (dangerous without discipline)
- **Special Situation Investing** — merger arb, spin-offs, turnarounds
- **Technical Analysis** — price/momentum-driven

> "The Dean of Valuation (Damodaran) goes wrong with *when* to buy and *when* to sell."

### What Valuation Is and Is Not
- Valuation is **partly art, partly science**.
- Best valuation exercise = **fewest assumptions, least time spent** (Warren Buffett never built a spreadsheet model — he handicapped on 1-2 variables).
- "You have to be **roughly right** and **not precisely wrong**."
- "Good businesses at wrong prices don't make for good investments." — Prashant Jain

### What Really Matters in Valuation
| Factor | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| Earnings Growth | Primary driver of stock price over time |
| Management Quality | Determines capital allocation and governance |
| Margin Variance | Is the company over- or under-earning? |
| B2B End-User Health | If customer is suffering, revenue is at risk |
| Working Capital & FCF | Cash quality of earnings |
| Return on Capital Employed | True economic return |
| Quality of Revenue | Is it improving or declining? |
| Competitive Intensity | New entrants erode terminal value |
| Governance | Non-negotiable filter |
| Ability to Add Verticals | "Epic stock" characteristic |
| Float / Public Shareholding | Liquidity and price discovery |
| Size of Opportunity (TAM) | Runway for growth |

## 2. Valuation Metrics — Definitions and Formulas

### Price to Earnings (P/E)
**Formula:** Market Cap / Net Profit (PAT)

**Use when:**
- Stable earnings businesses (specialty chemicals, FMCG, paints, IT)
- No one-off distortions in earnings

**Do NOT use when:**
- One-off items (Exide, Aarti Industries examples)
- Company is in losses / turnaround
- Life Insurance, Banks, Real Estate
- Operating leverage play (pre-utilization)
- Deep cyclicals
- Cornered float with massive upcoming growth (be lenient)
- Companies adding multiple new growth avenues simultaneously

**Core P/E Calculation:**
> Take PAT and subtract "Other Income" (treasury income, one-offs not core to business). This gives true operating PE.

**TTM P/E vs. Forward P/E:**
- TTM (Trailing Twelve Months): historical
- Forward PE = Current Market Cap / Estimated Next Year Earnings
- Use Forward PE when company is in investment phase (pharma, textiles post-capex)

---

### Price to Earnings Growth (PEG Ratio)
**Formula:** P/E Ratio / Earnings Growth Rate (%)

**TTM PEG:** Use last 3 or 5 years of earnings growth
**Forward PEG:** Use 1-year or 2-year forward earnings growth estimate

| PEG Level | Signal |
|-----------|--------|
| < 1.0x | **Deep Value** — market expecting no growth; growth can surprise; PE re-rating opportunity |
| < 1.5x | **GARP** — Growth at Reasonable Price; good risk/reward |
| 1.5x – 2.0x | **Caution zone** — pricing in significant optimism |
| > 2.0x | **Danger zone** — you are discounting many years ahead; trouble ahead |

**PEG Examples from Webinar:**
- Stock A: PE 30x, Growth 20% → PEG = 1.5x (GARP borderline)
- Stock B: PE 60x, Growth 10% → PEG = 6.0x (extreme danger)
- Stock C: PE 20x, Growth 20% → PEG = 1.0x (deep value/GARP entry)

> "When you start discounting prices many years ahead, you know there is trouble ahead."

---

### EV/EBITDA
**Formula:** Enterprise Value / EBITDA
- EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash
- EBITDA = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization

**Use when:**
- Asset-heavy businesses with high depreciation (Hospitals, Hotels, Telecom, Cement)
- Too many one-offs in earnings
- Assessing payback if a private acquirer buys the company today
- Companies in loss at PAT level but positive at EBITDA level

**Samosa Shop Example:** EV = 5.5 Cr, EBITDA = 1 Cr → EV/EBITDA = 5.5x = buying at 5.5 years of operating profit payback.

---

### Price to Cash Flow (P/CFO)
**Formula:** Market Cap / Operating Cash Flow (CFO)

**Use when:**
- High depreciation industries (hospitals, capital goods)
- Working capital is manageable
- Companies undergoing high capex (real earnings depressed optically)

**EV/CFO:** Enterprise Value / CFO — check payback in number of years using cash flows.

---

### Price to Free Cash Flow (P/FCF)
**Formula:** Market Cap / (CFO − Capex)

**Use for:**
- Platform businesses, exchanges — companies that throw off cash without needing much capital
- IT sector (light capex, high FCF generation)

**EV/FCF:** EV / FCF — for capital-light businesses

---

### Price to Book Value (P/BV)
**Formula:** Market Cap / Net Worth (Equity + Free Reserves, excluding revaluation reserves)

**Why used for Banks:**
- Bank's core engine is the spread game (difference between cost of funds and yield on loans)
- P/BV captures fund utilization efficiency better than P/E
- Basel regulations require banks to maintain core capital as % of asset book → P/BV becomes proxy for effective yields
- HDFC Bank and Kotak Bank earn premium P/BV because they manage NPAs and spreads better — not because they are "undervalued"

**Bank KPIs that drive P/BV:**
1. Book Value per Share (BVPS) — compounds at ROE rate
2. Return on Assets (ROA)
3. Return on Equity (ROE)

---

### Price to Sales (P/S or Mcap/Sales)
**Formula:** Market Cap / Revenue

**Use when:**
- Company has negative earnings / EBITDA (early stage, investment phase)
- SaaS / tech businesses at losses — bet on margin expansion + revenue growth
- Manufacturing companies where margins can improve
- Auto ancillaries in loss phase
- Cyclicals at trough

**Key insight:** When you use P/S, you are betting on TWO things simultaneously:
1. Sales will grow
2. EBITDA margins will expand

**SaaS example:** Best SaaS companies trade at 10x P/S; worst at 1x P/S. Assign a multiple based on margin potential and growth.

---

### EV/Tonne (Cement)
**Use:** Compare cement companies to the replacement cost of setting up new capacity
- If EV/Tonne << replacement cost → potential value unlocking
- Helps assess M&A attractiveness

---

### Order Book / Market Cap (EPC / Capital Goods / Defence)
**Formula:** Total Outstanding Order Book / Market Cap of company

**What it tells you:**
- How many years of revenue is already locked in
- Discount/premium relative to market cap
- Order Book / Sales = sustainability of current revenue run rate

**EPC business full checklist:**
1. Order book size and growth
2. Promoter track record
3. Government budget allocation for the sub-sector
4. Order Book / Market Cap ratio
5. Cash flow health and balance sheet
6. Geographies of operation
7. Margins earned on orders

---

### Sum of the Parts (SOTP)
**Used when:** Conglomerate with multiple unrelated businesses (like a biscuit company with cream-filled + chocolate chip + salted varieties)
- Value each business segment separately using its relevant multiple
- Add up → total intrinsic value

**When to use SOTP:**
- Holding companies
- Diversified conglomerates
- Companies with listed + unlisted subsidiaries
- Businesses with fundamentally different growth profiles in same entity

---

### Price to Embedded Value (Life Insurance)
- Embedded Value = economic value of in-force business + net asset value
- Life insurance companies are valued on P/EV (Price to Embedded Value) — not PE or PBV
- Not applicable to general insurance

---

### Mcap / Pre-Sales (Real Estate)
- Real estate P&L is meaningless (revenue only booked on project delivery, 2-3 years later)
- Track: Pre-sales (bookings), Collections, CFO — not reported revenue
- Key metric: Mcap / Embedded EBITDA or Mcap / Pre-sales

---

### EV/GCI (Gross Capital Invested)
- Less commonly discussed but useful for capital-intensive businesses
- Compares enterprise value to total capital deployed (equity + debt invested in the business)

## 4. Key Business KPIs by Sector

### Hospitals
- **EV/EBITDA** is the primary metric (high depreciation from beds, equipment)
- Key variables: New bed additions (brownfield cheaper than greenfield), occupancy rates
- New centers drag margins initially → watch for ramp-up trajectory
- HCG case: EBITDA margins of new centers much lower than mature centers — watch consolidation

### IT Sector
- P/E + free cash flow generation
- Large-cap IT above 25x PE = risky (e.g., Infosys at 206x in 2000 = disaster)
- Small/mid-cap IT with 30-40% growth can justify higher PE
- Key KPIs: Client concentration (top 5-10), attrition rate, margin variability, guidance

### Banks
**10 Things to Track:**
1. Asset Quality — GNPAs & NNPAs
2. Book Value per Share
3. ROA / ROE
4. Management continuity
5. Don't over-rely on guidance (very macro-dependent)
6. Opex investment made by bank
7. Cost-to-income ratio
8. Secured vs. Unsecured lending mix
9. Advances growth vs. deposit growth
10. RBI actions (banning lending in a segment)

**ROE Compounding math:**
- Bank earning 10% ROE: Equity compounds slowly (100 → 110 → 121...)
- Bank earning 20% ROE: Equity compounds fast (100 → 120 → 144...)
- This is why HDFC Bank/Kotak command 3-4x PBV vs weak banks at 0.5x

### Real Estate
**P&L is useless** — only track:
- Pre-sales (bookings in current period)
- Collections against bookings
- Inventory on balance sheet
- CFO (Cash Flow from Operations) — "track like a hawk"
- Annuity income (from rental/commercial portfolios)
- Debt trajectory
- Land bank value
- Geography — cities where demand > supply show faster ASP appreciation

**Valuation:** Mcap/Pre-sales, Mcap/Embedded EBITDA, or implied margin on pre-sold projects

### CDMO (Contract Development and Manufacturing)
- Structural growth sector — highest valuation among pharma sub-sectors
- Key distinction: Which innovator companies are clients? (Pfizer, Eli Lilly > Glenmark)
- Pipeline quality: How many molecules in Phase 2 vs. commercial?
- CDMO-C (multiple drugs commercial + 3 in pipeline) > CDMO-B (1 drug commercial) > CDMO-A (Indian pharma clients)
- Metric: EV/EBITDA, Price/Sales, Margins

### Auto Sector
**Key variables:**
1. Industry cycle position (3-4 years growth, 2-3 years downturn)
2. Product strategy — SUV launches matter enormously (Mahindra example)
3. Market share gains
4. Volume growth (double-digit vs. single-digit)
5. Sales mix: Hatchback vs. SUV + export contribution
6. Agri sector exposure

**Valuation:** Compare P/E vs. Auto sector index; EV/EBITDA at cycle bottom is useful

### Cement
**Key variables:**
1. EBITDA per tonne (shows pricing power and cost efficiency)
2. Backward integration capability
3. Balance sheet strength for acquisitions
4. Capacity expansion plans
5. Regional ASP (varies significantly by region)
6. Market share trajectory

**Valuation:** EV/EBITDA + EV/Tonne vs. replacement cost of new plant (typically Rs. 100-150 Cr/MT)

### Alcohol
**Key variables:**
1. Premiumisation journey — how far along from IMFL → semi-premium → premium
2. Millionaire brands (brands selling 1 million+ cases/year)
3. IMFL vs. Contract Manufacturing vs. Ethanol mix
4. Volume growth by category
5. Geographic presence

**Quality hierarchy:** 100% branded (Company C) > 70% branded (Company A) > mixed with ethanol/contract (Company B)

**Valuation:** PEG + PE vs. volume growth, premiumisation stage

### Capital Goods
**Key variables:**
1. Order book growth (visibility for 2-3 years)
2. EBITDA margins
3. Operating leverage (capacity utilization)
4. End-industry trends (customer capex cycle)
5. Risk of customer backward integration

**Valuation:** Forward PE, Bull/Base/Bear scenarios; capacity utilization is the trigger for margin expansion

### EPC / Infrastructure
**8 things to check:**
1. Order book size
2. Promoter past track record
3. Government budget allocation for sub-sector
4. Order Book / Market Cap
5. Cash flows and balance sheet health
6. Geographies of presence
7. Order book growth rate
8. Margins on current orders

**Valuation multiples:** PE, P/CFO, Order Book/Mcap, Order Book/Sales

### Platforms / New-Age
- Loss-making stage: Use Price/Sales
- Profitable: P/FCF, PE
- Exit multiple: 1.5x–2x above index PE due to scalability premium
- Track: Revenue growth, margin inflection timeline, CAC, LTV

### Wealth Management vs. Broker
- Broker: Highly cyclical (40-50% profit decline in downturns), gets lower valuation
- Wealth Management: Counter-cyclical revenue mix (debt products in downturns), more stable, commands higher valuation

## 5. PEG Ratio — Deep Dive and Thresholds

### Formula
PEG = (P/E Ratio) / (EPS Growth Rate %)

### The Odds Meter
| Valuation Zone | PEG | What's Happening | Risk/Reward |
|---------------|-----|------------------|-------------|
| **Deep Value** | < 1.0x | Market expects zero/negative growth. Growth can positively surprise → PE re-rating | Favourable |
| **GARP** | < 1.5x | Paying a fair price for growth | Reasonable |
| **Caution** | 1.5x – 2.0x | Some future optimism priced in | Neutral to risky |
| **Danger** | > 2.0x | Discounting multiple years of future growth | Unfavourable |

### Samir Arora's Quote on Paints
> "Paints company was at 30-40x earnings not so long back. Earnings growth is still the same, yet PE has increased to 90-100x. Making odds unfavourable."

When PE expands without earnings growth acceleration, the PEG has ballooned into danger zone.

### How to Calculate
- **TTM PEG:** Use last 3 or 5 years average PAT growth rate
- **Forward PEG:** Use analyst estimates for next 1-2 years
- For high-growth phase companies (like Evolution Gaming), use forward PEG

## 6. Over-Earning / Under-Earning Concept

### Definition
**Over-earning:** A company earns profits significantly above its long-run normalized average, due to:
- Temporary shortages in its product
- One-off orders / windfall opportunities
- Commodity price spikes favouring it
- Supply disruptions in the industry (e.g., China shutdowns)

### Why It's Dangerous
When a company over-earns:
1. Market extrapolates peak earnings → awards peak PE → **massive overvaluation**
2. When earnings normalize, the base is too high → "growth" disappears optically
3. Market reacts brutally to even small earnings misses

> "Over earnings leads to the creation of massive expectations and a large base size."

### Indian Examples of Over-Earning
| Company/Sector | Over-Earning Driver | Result |
|----------------|--------------------|----|
| Divis Laboratories | Molnupiravir (COVID drug) | Margins collapsed post-COVID |
| Laurus Labs | Paxlovid ingredient supply | Stock corrected sharply post-peak |
| Aarti Industries | One-off income in chemicals | PE compression when normalised |
| PVC Pipe Companies | Raw material shortage + spread | Margins normalised, stocks fell |
| Amine Players | DMF prices spiked | Concall showed normalisation |
| Apcotex | Over-earning → normal → under-earning | Three-stage deterioration |
| Balaji Amines | DMF prices + margins spike | Multi-year margin compression |

### Apcotex Case
The company went through a full cycle:
1. Over-earning (super-normal margins)
2. Normal margins
3. Under-earning (below historical average)
Key warning signs: Margins going below normal AND staying there + Inventory losses appearing

### Signs a Company is Over-Earning
1. Margins significantly above 5-10 year historical average WITHOUT product mix change
2. Industry capacity additions accelerating (attracted by high returns)
3. Peer companies all reporting exceptional profits simultaneously
4. Management cannot explain *why* margins are high structurally

### Mean Reversion Application
1. Always look at **long-term margins** (5-10 year average)
2. If margins are running 2x+ historical → investigate
3. Consider normalised earnings, not peak earnings, for valuation

## 7. Zone of Danger

The single most important concept for avoiding catastrophic losses:

### The Zone of Danger Triangle

```
         PEAK P/E
            /\
           /  \
          /    \
PEAK      \    /   PEAK
MARGINS    \  /    EARNINGS
            \/
     ZONE OF DANGER
```

**All three simultaneously = maximum risk:**
- **Peak P/E:** Market is euphoric, willing to pay maximum multiple
- **Peak Margins:** Operating profitability at cyclical/structural high
- **Peak Earnings:** Absolute profit level at multi-year high

### Why This Is Lethal
When **all three** are at peaks simultaneously, **only one negative surprise** is needed for de-rating to begin:
- Margins compress → earnings fall → PE contracts
- Three-way compression = stock can fall 60-80% even from a "good company"

### Examples of Zone of Danger Entry Points
| Sector | Year | All Three at Peak? | Outcome |
|--------|------|-------------------|---------|
| IT Sector | 2000 | Yes (PE 200x+, peak margins) | 80% crash |
| Infrastructure | 2008 | Yes | Decade of pain |
| Pharma (US-facing) | 2015 | Yes | Multi-year underperformance |
| NBFCs | 2018 | Yes | IL&FS crisis |
| API Stocks (Divis, Laurus) | 2021 | Yes | 50-70% corrections |
| Paints | 2021-22 | Yes (PE 90-100x) | De-rating began |

### How to Avoid Zone of Danger
1. Check current margins vs. 5-year average — if current >> average, be cautious
2. Check current PE vs. historical PE range — buying at high end of range increases risk
3. Check earnings trajectory — at cyclical peak or structural high?
4. If all three signal "high," reduce position / avoid new entry

## 8. Terminal Value Framework

### Why Terminal Value Matters
> "All Investing is about Terminal Value." — Utpal Sheth

In a standard DCF, the terminal value can account for **60% of total present value**. This is the "longevity" component of the valuation.

> "The P/E you could have paid to beat the index for high quality." — Terry Smith

### What Terminal Value Represents
It is the answer to: **How long will this business sustain competitive advantage and generate returns above cost of capital?**

A high-quality, durable business deserves a higher terminal value (higher PE) than a mediocre, disruption-prone business — even if near-term earnings are similar.

### Characteristics of EXPANDING Terminal Value
(These are the "Epic Stock" characteristics that justify premium multiples)

| Characteristic | Why It Matters |
|---------------|---------------|
| Superb capital allocation | ROIC stays high; growth creates value, not destroys it |
| Clean accounting | No off-balance-sheet games; earnings quality is real |
| Long runway for growth | Large TAM + low penetration = decades of growth ahead |
| Consumable product / repeat purchases | Built-in recurring revenue; customer churn is low |
| Very low risk of obsolescence | Product not being disrupted by technology or regulation |

### What DESTROYS Terminal Value
1. **Technology disruption** — e.g., Lead-acid battery companies (Exide, Amara Raja) vs. EV batteries where OEMs want to manufacture in-house
2. **Regulatory risk** — government policy can eliminate a moat overnight
3. **Commoditization** — when a "specialty" product becomes commodity (many specialty chemical companies)
4. **Competition entering** — Symphony example: high ROIIC attracted competitors → ROIIC fell from 75% to -25% in 5 years
5. **Promoter governance issues** — destroys trust, hence multiple
6. **Structural industry decline** — company's end-market shrinking

### Symphony (Air Coolers) — Competition Destroying Terminal Value
| Year | ROIIC |
|------|-------|
| 2016 | 75% |
| 2017 | 63% |
| 2018 | 53% |
| 2019 | 5% |
| 2020 | -25% |

Lesson: "The nature of the game is that your great businesses are being eroded by something at all times." — Charlie Munger

### DCF Components
1. **Growth in Cash Flows:** How fast do they grow? Any positive surprises possible?
2. **Discount Rate:** Depends on certainty of cash flows and where business is in growth journey
3. **Terminal Value:** How sustainable is the business? This is where longevity (moats) matters

### Discount Rate
- Higher certainty of cash flows → lower discount rate → higher valuation
- Higher interest rates → higher discount rate → lower valuation (gravity metaphor)
- Buffett's personal hurdle: **15% annual return minimum from Day 1**

## 9. DCF — How Buffett Actually Does It

### The Mental Model (from Schroeder's "The Snowball")
Buffett does NOT build spreadsheet DCF models. Instead, he:

1. **First asks: what can I lose?** (Risk-first thinking, opposite of most investors)
2. **Isolates 1-2 key variables** (like a horse handicapper) — not 50 variables
3. **Studies long-term historical data** laid out on legal pad
4. **Sets 15% as hurdle rate** — if business can return 15% from Day 1, invest

### Mid-Continent Tab Card Company Case Study
- 1950s: IBM forced to divest tab card business (50% profit margins)
- Buffett initially declined (start-up risk)
- Later invited again when: 40% profit margins, machine payback in 1 year, 36% margins + 70% growth
- Simple question: "Can I get 15% return from this price?" → YES → Invested $60,000 (20% of net worth)
- Held 18 years, earned 33% CAGR total return
- "A Phil Fisher investment at a Ben Graham price"

### Key Principle
> "Buffett simply looks at detailed long-term historical data and determines, based on the price he has to pay, if he can get at least a 15% return. This is how Buffett does a discounted cash flow. There are no discounted cash flow models." — Alice Schroeder

### Buffett's DCF Framework (from Aesop)
"A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush." — answer three questions:
1. How certain are you that there ARE birds in the bush?
2. When will they emerge, and how many?
3. What is the risk-free interest rate?

If you can answer all three with confidence, you know the maximum price to pay.

### Compounding Tables (Reference)
**Rs. 1 Lakh at different rates:**
| Rate | Year 1 | Year 5 | Year 10 |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| 5% | 1,05,000 | 1,27,628 | 1,62,889 |
| 10% | 1,10,000 | 1,61,051 | 2,59,374 |
| 15% | 1,15,000 | 2,01,135 | 4,04,555 |
| 20% | 1,20,000 | 2,48,832 | 6,19,173 |
| 25% | 1,25,000 | 3,05,175 | 9,31,322 |

**Why 15% matters:** At 15%, Rs 1 lakh becomes Rs 4 lakh in 10 years. At 20%, it becomes Rs 6.2 lakh. This difference justifies Buffett's strict hurdle.

### Importance of Terminal Value in DCF
Typical DCF breakdown:
- Present Value (explicit forecast period): ~40%
- Terminal Value: ~60%

**Implication:** Getting the terminal value right is MORE important than getting near-term earnings forecasts right. A business with durable moats deserves a higher TV → justifies higher PE today.

## 10. Interest Rates as Gravity on Valuations

### Buffett's Gravity Quote
> "Interest rates are like gravity in valuations. If interest rates are nothing, values can be almost infinite. If interest rates are extremely high, that's a huge gravitational pull on values." — Warren Buffett

**Simple version:**
- **High interest rates → Lower stock valuations**
- **Low interest rates → Higher stock valuations**

### Mechanism: What Happens When Rates Rise
1. **Discount rates increase** → Future cash flows worth less today → Lower intrinsic value
2. **PE multiples contract** → Market re-prices even good businesses at lower multiples
3. **Bonds become attractive** once stabilised → Capital flows from equities to bonds
4. **New investments become tougher** → Corporate capex slows → Earnings growth slows

### The PE Contraction Effect
If a business earns Rs 10 EPS and was trading at 50x PE (Rs 500) when rates were at 4%:
- Rates rise to 7%
- Same business now trades at 30x PE → Rs 300
- Stock falls 40% even though earnings didn't change

This is exactly what happened in 2022 globally.

### Historical Evidence: Volcker's Rate Hikes (1978-1981)
- Fed raised rates by 100 bps seven times between Nov 1978 and May 1981
- Markets fell ~60% of the time in the month after a 100-bps hike
- S&P lost average 2.4% one month after hike; still down 1.3% after three months
- By six-month mark, leveled out (+0.1%)
- Inflation went from 9% (Nov 1978) → peak 14.6% (Mar 1980)
- Despite S&P gaining 25% in 1980, rate hikes eventually caused 1981-82 recession

### India Application
- Post-COVID rate hikes (2022-23) caused compression of high-PE sectors
- NIFTY Quality 30 PE fell from 33x+ to 26x
- Sectors most affected: New-age tech, platform businesses, high-PE specialty plays

### For Indian Investors
- RBI rate cycle matters for Nifty PE
- When RBI is in cutting cycle → expansion of multiples
- When RBI is hawkish → compression of multiples
- Market Cap to GDP ratio is a proxy for whether market is expensive vs. cheap (Buffett indicator)
- Buffett bought in Fall 2008 when market was at 70-80% of GDP

## 11. Cyclicality Classification

### The Three Tiers

#### Deep Cyclical (High Volatility, Wide Swings)
| Sector | Why Cyclical |
|--------|-------------|
| Fertilizers | Dependent on agri cycle, subsidy policies |
| Auto Ancillaries | Follows auto volumes (3-4 year cycles) |
| Commodity Chemicals | Price driven by global supply/demand |
| Commodity APIs | API prices volatile (Chinese supply, FDA) |
| Generic Pharma (US-facing) | Pricing erosion + ANDA cycles |
| Shipping | Shipping rates extremely volatile (BDI) |
| Regional Cement Companies | Local demand cycles |
| Most NBFCs | Credit cycle dependent |
| Metals | Commodity pricing, China demand |
| Agri Produce | Monsoon + commodity prices |
| Oil & Gas | Crude oil price dependency |

**Valuation at cycle bottom:** EV/Sales or P/Sales (P/E not meaningful at trough)

#### Shallow Cyclical (Moderate Volatility)
| Sector | Why Shallow |
|--------|------------|
| Tyres | Follows auto sector but with lag |
| Auto | Volume cycles but structural growth |
| Domestic Pharma | Branded, but subject to regulatory cycles |
| Specialty Chemicals | More pricing power but some cyclicality |
| Off-The-Highway Tyres | Agri + infra exposure |
| Agrochemicals | Monsoon + seasonal |
| Some Building Materials | Construction cycle |
| Niche businesses (CDSL) | Some regulatory/market cycle |

#### Structural (Low Cyclicality)
| Sector | Why Structural |
|--------|---------------|
| CDMOs | Long-term innovation drug pipeline |
| FMCG | Staples, repeat consumption |
| Consumer Durables | Upgradation cycle, not reversal |

**Structural businesses deserve highest PE multiples** because terminal value is most predictable.

### How to Invest in Deep Cyclicals
1. Go to sectors that haven't performed for 3-5 years (screener: 5-year stock return near 0)
2. Look at sectoral P/E ratios — buying when PE is low in cycle
3. Be careful: ensure the business will survive long-run (avoid companies with balance sheet risk)
4. Use EV/Sales or P/Sales at cycle troughs, not P/E

## 13. Case Studies

### Infosys — The Entry Point Matters
**PE History:**
- Year 2000: 206x P/E (bubble peak)
- Normalized range: 14x-34x (2003-2022)

**PAT Growth History:**
- Early 2000s: 115%-125% growth
- 2008-2016: Slowdown to 7%-18%
- 2020+: Recovery to 15-22%

**Returns Based on Entry Point:**
| Entry | Exit | CAGR |
|-------|------|------|
| Mar 2000 (Rs.223) | Mar 2005 (Rs.177) | -5% CAGR |
| Mar 2001 (Rs.113) | Mar 2006 (Rs.194) | +12% CAGR |
| Mar 2002 (Rs.105) | Mar 2007 (Rs.240) | +18% CAGR |

**Lesson:** Same company, dramatically different returns based purely on **starting valuation**. The 2000 buyer paid 206x PE and lost money for 5 years; the 2002 buyer paid ~20x PE and made 18% CAGR.

**EBITDA Margins:**

- Q2FY22: 17% (mature centres recovering)

|------|-------|-----------|
| FY17 | 1,073 | Nagpur, Mumbai Borivali |
| FY18 | 1,923 | Jaipur, Rajkot |
| FY19 | 1,956 | Kolkata, South Mumbai |
| FY20 | 2,885 | |
| FY21 | 3,184 | Hubli |

**Scenario Analysis (FY2026E):**
| Scenario | EBITDA | Multiple | Market Cap |
|----------|--------|----------|-----------|
| Bear | Rs.350 Cr | 15x Mcap/EBITDA | Rs.5,200 Cr |
| Base | Rs.384 Cr | 20x | Rs.7,600 Cr |

**Key lesson:** New centre economics vs. mature centre economics must be tracked separately. Fresh hospitals drag consolidated margins for 3-4 years before turning positive.

### APL Apollo Tubes — ROCE Expansion Story
**Three levers for ROCE expansion:**
1. Net working capital reducing (receivables: Rs.476 Cr → Rs.124 Cr)
2. Deleveraging (debt: Rs.788 Cr FY20 → Rs.297 Cr by Q2FY21)
3. Entering value-added products (higher-margin product mix)

**Business model strength:**
- Largest purchaser of HRC (Hot Rolled Coil) in India → procurement advantage
- Just-in-time inventory → low working capital
- Extensive distribution → lower freight costs + scale
- Industry moved to cash model during COVID → receivables collapsed

**Key insight for low-margin, high capital businesses:** Competitors without balance sheet strength perish in tough periods → reduces competitive intensity → survivors gain market share + pricing power.

---

### KEI Industries — Working Capital Management
**Working Capital = Trade Receivables + Inventory − Trade Payables**

**Retention Money** (relevant for EPC/infra cable companies): Money held by client as defect safeguard — this appears as receivable but is NOT at risk of non-payment; just delayed.

**Channel Finance Innovation:** KEI moved from 100% recourse channel finance (must report as receivable) to 50-75% non-recourse channel finance → receivables shrink → working capital improves → ROCE improves → stock re-rated.

---

### IRCTC — Government Monopoly ROCE
**ROCE Trajectory:**
| Year | ROCE |
|------|------|
| 2016 | 17% |
| 2017 | 40% |
| 2018 | 44% |
| 2019 | 54% |
| 2020 | 62% |
| 2021 | 63% |

IRCTC commands very high PE as a PSU monopoly because ROCE is exceptional and capital is minimal (no physical assets needed). The valuation debate is entirely about regulatory risk (government taking back internet ticketing commission).

---

### Laurus Labs — Operating Leverage Play
**Con-call evidence (pre-earnings explosion):**

Q4FY18: "Gross block of Rs.2,000 Cr with 1:1 asset turnover. More than 40% of assets not yielding any revenue. In next 2 financial years, will be fully utilized."

Q1FY19: "~Rs.600 Cr of assets generating no revenue (Formulation Unit 4, 6, Unit 5). Once revenue starts from these units, ROA and ROCE will automatically improve."

**Lesson:** This is the signal for **operating leverage**. When fixed costs are already spent, incremental revenue flows almost entirely to profit. The PAT/EBITDA surge looks like "super-earnings" but is really the culmination of earlier investment.

**Warning:** When capacity is fully utilized and the specific drug cycle ends (Paxlovid), over-earning begins → be cautious.

---

### Astral Pipes — "Always Expensive" Compounder
- Astral was always expensive — never "cheap" by standard metrics
- Yet it was a massive multi-bagger because: capacity kept expanding, demand grew, ROCE remained high
- Key learning: For compounders with genuine long runway, PE compression waiting strategy can mean missing the entire move

**Capacity Utilization Journey:**
- 2006: 2,417 MT capacity
- 2020: 2,38,730 MT capacity
- ~100x capacity addition over 14 years → revenue grew proportionally

---

### Navin Fluorine — Re-rating on Capex Announcement
- Re-rating happened on back of **large capex announcements** (indicative of pipeline growth)
- Market pays up in anticipation of future earnings from new capex
- Risk: Capex must actually execute and deliver margins → watch actual commissioning dates and revenue from new plants

---

### Balaji Amines — Over-Earning and Reversion
- DMF (Di-methyl Formamide) prices spiked → massive margin expansion
- Q3FY22 concall showed normalisation of DMF prices beginning
- Company had also diversified into capital-heavy Hotels and CFL business (capital employed in non-core areas)
- Margins reverted → stock corrected sharply
- Lesson: Always separate core business margins from corporate capital allocation

---

### Hero Motocorp — "Value Remains Value Without Growth"
- Hero was consistently profitable, good dividends, cheap PE
- But growth had stalled — market share stable, EV threat emerging
- Quote from SOIC: **"Value remains value if there is no growth."**
- Without earnings growth, PE compression cannot be averted by cheap headline valuation
- Stock was a value trap for years despite apparent cheapness

---

### Tata Elxsi — Three Investor Types
Three friends, same stock:
1. Bought at 700, sold at 1,500 (thought expensive) → missed 6x further upside
2. Bought at 1,000, rode to 9,000 → couldn't sell (greed) → gave back gains eventually
3. Bought at 1,000, sold at 8,800 → knew when to exit (technicals helped)

**Lesson:** Exits are the most important decision. Technicals can teach *when* to exit even when fundamentals remain intact.

### Battery Companies (Exide / Amara Raja) — Terminal Value Destruction
- EV battery: costs up to 50% of total vehicle cost vs. fraction for lead-acid
- Automakers choosing to manufacture batteries in-house
- Bargaining power of Exide/Amara Raja with OEMs **structurally changing**
- Massive investments planned by both companies could become obsolete
- Terminal value is being destroyed → sell signal

**Quote:** "Such stocks, at least in my framework, are easy sell decisions as the business road is too uncertain in these cases."

### Vinati Organics — Crude Oil Prices as Key Variable
- Narrows down to one variable: Crude oil as raw material input
- When crude spikes → input costs up → margins compress
- When crude falls → margins expand
- The "horse handicapping" approach — one variable to watch closely

### Japan Bubble Analogy
- Japanese real estate/asset bubble (1980s): Reflexivity in action
- Price rises → perception improves → fundamentals appear stronger → price rises more
- Vicious cycle in reverse: Price falls → perception worsens → forced selling → further falls
- Recovery from Japanese bubble took 30+ years

### Theory of Reflexivity (George Soros)
```
PRICE UP → PERCEPTION IMPROVES → FUNDAMENTALS APPEAR STRONGER → PRICE FURTHER UP
(Virtuous cycle — bubble forming)

PRICE DOWN → PERCEPTION WORSENS → FUNDAMENTALS APPEAR WEAKER → PRICE FURTHER DOWN
(Vicious cycle — bubble bursting)
```

### Every Bubble Ends with Liquidity Tightening
Bubbles don't burst from fundamental analysis; they burst when central banks withdraw liquidity:
- US 1928-29: Fed tightened → Black Tuesday
- Japan 1989: Bank of Japan tightened → 30-year decline
- India 1992: Harshad scam exposed → liquidity withdrawn
- US 2000: Fed raised rates → Nasdaq -80%
- India 2018: NBFC crisis → IL&FS collapse

## 15. 10 Common Valuation Mistakes

Synthesized from all three webinars:

### Mistake 1: Using Wrong Metric for the Sector
Applying PE to a hospital (should be EV/EBITDA), or PBV to an IT company (should be PE). The report card analogy: using Sahil's overall 75% score when you should be looking at his 98% science score.

### Mistake 2: Confusing Peak Earnings for Normal Earnings
The over-earning trap. Paying peak PE on peak earnings at peak margins = Zone of Danger. Always normalize earnings before applying a multiple.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Starting Valuations
Infosys lesson: same quality company, wildly different returns based on entry PE. A 206x PE entry leads to -5% CAGR; a 20x PE entry leads to +18% CAGR.

### Mistake 4: Extrapolating Near-Term Earnings Too Far
"When you start discounting prices many years ahead, you know there is trouble ahead." PEG > 2x = you're paying for 5-7 years of perfect execution.

### Mistake 5: Not Adjusting for One-Offs in PE
Core PE must subtract other income (treasury/non-operating). Otherwise the PE looks artificially low while actual operating PE is much higher. Examples: Exide, Aarti Industries.

### Mistake 6: Using PE for Deep Cyclicals at Cycle Peak
At the top of a commodity/chemical cycle, PE looks low (high earnings). But it is actually the most expensive moment to buy. Use EV/Sales at cycle bottom, not PE at cycle peak.

### Mistake 7: Ignoring Terminal Value Risks
Battery company example: near-term earnings fine, but EV disruption destroys terminal value. A stock can be "cheap" on next 2-3 year earnings yet be a permanent value trap if terminal value is heading to zero.

### Mistake 8: SOTP Without Haircut
Sum-of-the-parts math often values each segment at full market multiple. But holding company structures command 20-40% discount to NAV. Failing to apply haircut leads to overestimated value.

### Mistake 9: Catching a Falling Knife
"Never catch a falling knife." For value investors, waiting for base formation (higher lows forming, moving averages stabilising) significantly improves outcomes. "Sometimes the bottom is bottomless."

### Mistake 10: Valuing Only on Current PE Without Looking at Business Quality
Hero Motocorp: appeared cheap for years on PE. But zero growth = value trap. Valuations must always be combined with growth outlook and ROCE trajectory.

### Bonus Mistake: Justifying High PE With a Story
Every cycle, market justifies high valuations with narratives:
- "IT stocks at 300 PE in 1999 because the internet will change everything"
- "Real estate at any price in 2007 because India will grow"
- "NBFCs at 10x book in 2018 because India is underbanked"
Each narrative was true directionally; none justified the price paid.

## 16. Mean Reversion and Capital Returns

### Mean Reversion Theory
> "Asset price volatility and historical returns eventually will revert to the long-run mean or average level of the entire dataset."

### Application in Investing
1. Always look at **long-term margins** (5-10 year data series)
2. If margins significantly above average WITHOUT product mix change → investigate over-earning
3. If margins significantly below average → investigate under-earning (potential opportunity)
4. Peak valuations + Peak Earnings + Peak Margins → Zone of Danger

### Capital Returns Framework (Capital Returns Book)
Key learnings:
- High ROIC attracts competition
- Competition erodes ROIC over time
- Industries with structural barriers maintain ROIC longer
- Low capital-intensity businesses can return capital instead of growing capex → creates value

## 17. Expectations Investing

### Concept
Instead of forecasting future cash flows (hard, error-prone), work **backwards** from the current stock price:
1. **Estimate Price-Implied Expectations (PIE):** Use current price + DCF model in reverse → what growth/margins is the market pricing?
2. **Identify Expectations Opportunities:** Where is the market's implied expectation wrong (either too optimistic or too pessimistic)?
3. **Make Buy/Sell/Hold decisions** based on gap between implied expectations vs. your view

### Why This Is Powerful
- Frees you from point forecasting
- Forces you to think about what's already "priced in"
- A great company at fair price = Hold (expectations met, no upside surprise)
- A mediocre company at terrible price = Buy if recovery in sight (expectations too low)

### Market Expectations Warning
Most investors use PE multiples as proxies — but PE multiples are "not reliably linked to shareholder value" and don't capture economic reality. The DCF/expectations framework is more rigorous.

| Learning | Application |
|---------|-------------|

| Investing in downturns | Fundamentals tell you when to buy fear |
| Avoiding shady managements | Technicals won't catch fraud; fundamentals help |
| Type of stock | Growth vs. value vs. deep value |

### Base Formation — Critical for Value Investors
**Why it matters:**
1. Time value of money — sitting in a falling stock has opportunity cost
2. "We don't know what is lying beyond the balance sheet"
3. Sometimes the bottom is bottomless

**Signs of base formation:**
- Higher lows forming (stop for 4-12 weeks)
- Lower lows formation stops
- Stock moves sideways for extended period
- Moving averages stabilise and turn flat

> "For value investors, **never catch a falling knife**."

### Valuation Multiple Collapse Triggers
1. **Sector enters strong headwinds** — e.g., NBFCs post-2017, Pharma post-2022
2. **Fraud comes out** — e.g., Solara, SKM Egg, Vakrangee
3. **History of problems** — e.g., Meghmani Group (governance + multiple business failures)
