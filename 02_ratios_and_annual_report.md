## 1. What is Ratio Analysis?

Ratio analysis is a way of gaining insight into a company's **capital allocation, liquidity, operational efficiency, and profitability** by studying its financial statements such as the balance sheet and income statement.

- Ratio analysis can **show how a company is performing over time**.
- It can be used to do a **peer-to-peer comparison** within the same industry or sector.
- Ratios are metrics which allow investors to evaluate the **historical track record** using various metrics — just like how we assess the statistics of Cricket players (e.g. strike rate), Football players (e.g. Goal Scoring Ratio), Tennis players (e.g. First Serve %), etc.

## 3. Activity Ratios (Efficiency Ratios)

An activity ratio is a type of financial metric that shows how **efficiently a company is leveraging the assets on its balance sheet** to generate revenues and cash.

- Also known as **efficiency ratios** — give an overview of how the company handles inventory management.
- Can be used to **compare two different businesses** within the same sector/industry, or to monitor a single company's fiscal health over time.

---

### 3.1 Inventory Turnover Ratio

**Definition:** Indicates the rate at which a company sells and replaces its stock of goods during a particular period. Helps businesses make better pricing, manufacturing, marketing, and purchasing decisions.

**Formulas:**
```
Inventory Turnover Ratio = COGS / Average Inventory
                      OR = Sales / Average Inventory

Average Inventory = (Beginning Inventory + Ending Inventory) / 2
```

---

### 3.2 Inventory Days

**Definition:** Indicates the average time in days that a company takes to turn its inventory (including goods that are work in progress) into sales. Indicates the **duration of time a company's cash is tied up in its inventory**.

**Formula:**
```
Inventory Days = Number of Days in a Period / Inventory Turnover
```

---

### 3.3 Receivable Turnover

**Definition:** Used to quantify a company's effectiveness in collecting its accounts receivable — the money owed by customers or clients. Measures how well a company uses and manages the credit it extends to customers and how quickly it is collected.

**Formula:**
```
Receivable Turnover = Sales / Average Receivables

Average Receivables = (Beginning Receivables + Ending Receivables) / 2
```

---

### 3.4 Receivable Days

**Definition:** A measure of the average number of days that it takes a company to collect payment for a sale. By quickly turning sales into cash, a company has a chance to put the cash to use again more quickly.

**Formula:**
```
Receivable Days = Number of Days in a Period / Receivable Turnover
```

---

### 3.5 Payable Turnover

**Definition:** A measure used to quantify the rate at which a company pays off its suppliers. Shows how efficient a company is at paying its suppliers and short-term debts.

**Formula:**
```
Payable Turnover = COGS / Average Payables

Average Payables = (Beginning Payables + Ending Payables) / 2
```

---

### 3.6 Payable Days

**Definition:** Same concept as payable turnover — measures how quickly a company pays its suppliers and short-term debts.

**Formula:**
```
Payable Days = Number of Days in a Period / Payable Turnover
```

---

### 3.7 Working Capital Turnover

**Definition:** A ratio that measures how efficiently a company is using its working capital to support sales and growth. Measures the relationship between the funds used to finance a company's operations and the revenues a company generates.

**Formula:**
```
Working Capital Turnover = Sales / Average Working Capital

Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
```

---

### 3.8 Total Assets Turnover

**Definition:** Measures the value of a company's sales or revenues relative to the value of its assets. An indicator of the efficiency with which a company is using its total assets to generate revenue.

**Formula:**
```
Total Assets Turnover = Sales / Average Total Assets
```

---

### 3.9 Fixed Assets Turnover

**Definition:** Used to measure operating performance. Compares net sales (income statement) to fixed assets (balance sheet) and measures a company's ability to generate net sales from its fixed-asset investments — namely property, plant, and equipment (PP&E).

**Formula:**
```
Fixed Asset Turnover = Sales / Average Fixed Assets
```

---

### 3.10 Gross Block Turnover

**Definition:** Tells us how much sales per unit of gross fixed assets the business is generating.

- **Gross Block** = the value of an asset **at cost** (depreciation NOT deducted)
- **Net Block** = Gross Block minus accrued depreciation

**Formula:**
```
Gross Block Turnover = Sales / Gross Block
```

**Company Example — Syngene (Gestation Period):**
- Syngene's Gross Fixed Asset Turns declined from 1x (2016) to 0.6x (2021) as gross block grew from Rs.1,111 Cr to Rs.4,450 Cr (2022E)
- This is a **gestation period** — Laurus Labs showed the same pattern: Net Fixed Asset Turns dipped from 2.3x (2014) to 1.4x (2019) before recovering to 2.2x (2021) as sales jumped from Rs.1,160 Cr to Rs.4,814 Cr
- Syngene is guiding for **1x asset turns**
- Key insight: **patience is required during heavy capex phases** before asset turns recover

## 4. Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios are an important class of financial metrics used to determine a **debtor's ability to pay off current debt obligations without raising external capital**.

- Measures how quickly assets are converted into cash.
- Determines a company's ability to cover **short-term obligations and cash flows**.

---

### 4.1 Current Ratio

**Definition:** Shows the company's ability to meet its short-term obligations within a period of one year. Tells investors how a company is utilizing its current assets to service its short-term debt and other obligations. Compares **all** current assets to current liabilities.

**Formula:**
```
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
```

---

### 4.2 Quick Ratio (Acid Test)

**Definition:** Indicates a company's ability to use its short-term cash assets to pay off its current liabilities. Excludes assets which cannot be converted to cash easily. Measures a company's capacity to pay its current liabilities **without needing to sell its inventory or obtain additional financing**.

**Formulas:**
```
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory - Prepaid Expenses) / Current Liabilities

          OR = (Cash + Trade Receivables + Money Market Securities) / Current Liabilities
```

---

### 4.3 Cash Ratio

**Definition:** A measurement of a company's liquidity — specifically the ratio of a company's total cash and cash equivalents to its current liabilities. Shows a company's ability to cover its short-term obligations using **only cash and cash equivalents**.

**Formula:**
```
Cash Ratio = Cash & Cash Equivalents / Current Liabilities
```

---

### 4.4 Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

**Definition:** A metric that expresses the time (measured in days) it takes for a company to convert its investments in inventory and other resources into **cash flows from sales**. Measures the length of the operating cycle.

**Formula:**
```
Cash Conversion Cycle = Inventory Days + Receivable Days - Payable Days
```

- A **lower (or negative) CCC** is better — means the company collects cash before it needs to pay suppliers (e.g. many FMCG and retail businesses)
- A **high CCC** means capital is tied up longer in operations

## 5. Solvency Ratios

A solvency ratio is a key metric used to measure a company's ability to meet its **long-term debt obligations**. Indicates whether a company's cash flow is sufficient to meet its long-term liabilities — a measure of financial health.

---

### 5.1 Debt-Equity Ratio (D/E)

**Definition:** A measure of the degree to which a company is financing its operations through debt versus wholly owned funds. Tells us the percentage of total assets that are financed by debt. Compares a company's total liabilities to its shareholder equity.

**Formula:**
```
Debt-Equity = (Long Term Debt + Short Term Debt) / Equity
```

---

### 5.2 Interest Coverage Ratio

**Definition:** Measures the margin of safety a company has for paying interest on its debt during a given period.

**Formula:**
```
Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense
```

---

### 5.3 Debt-Capital Ratio

**Definition:** A measurement of a company's financial leverage. All else being equal, the higher the debt-to-capital ratio, the riskier the company — because more of the company is funded by debt than equity, creating a higher liability to repay the debt.

**Formula:**
```
Debt-Capital = (Long Term Debt + Short Term Debt) / (Total Debt + Total Equity)
```

---

### 5.4 Debt-EBITDA Ratio

**Definition:** Measures the amount of income generated and available to pay down debt before covering interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization expenses. Measures a company's ability to pay off its incurred debt — shows how much actual cash flow the company has available to cover its debt.

**Formula:**
```
Debt/EBITDA = Debt / EBITDA
```

---

### 5.5 Financial Leverage

**Definition:** Tells us how much of the assets are being financed by equity. If total assets are 2 and equity is 1, then Rs.1 of equity supports Rs.2 worth of assets. Higher financial leverage means the company has taken more debt to finance assets. Financial leverage can **amplify earnings** — a % increase in sales leads to a disproportionate increase in EBIT.

**Formula:**
```
Financial Leverage = Total Assets / Equity
```

## 6. Profitability & Capital Allocation Ratios

Profitability ratios assess a company's ability to earn profits from its sales or operations, balance sheet assets, or shareholders' equity. They indicate how efficiently a company generates profit and value for shareholders.

---

### 6.1 Gross Profit Margin

**Definition:** A metric used to assess a company's financial health by calculating the amount of money left over from product sales after subtracting the cost of goods sold (COGS). Shows the amount of profit made **before** deducting selling, general, and administrative costs.

**Formula:**
```
Gross Profit Margin = (Gross Profit / Sales) x 100

Gross Profit = Sales - COGS
```

---

### 6.2 Operating Profit Margin (EBITDA Margin / OPM)

**Definition:** Measures how much profit a company makes on a rupee of sales after paying for variable costs of production (wages, raw materials) but before paying interest or tax. Represents how efficiently a company is able to generate profit through its core operations.

**Formula:**
```
Operating Profit Margin = (EBIT / Operating Profit / Sales) x 100

Operating Profit = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses - Depreciation - Amortization
```

---

### 6.3 Net Profit Margin (NPM / PAT Margin)

**Definition:** Measures how much net income or profit is generated as a percentage of revenue. The ratio of net profits to revenues for a company or business segment.

**Formula:**
```
Net Profit Margin = (Net Profit / Sales) x 100
```

---

### 6.4 Return on Assets (ROA)

**Definition:** An indicator of how well a company utilizes its assets in terms of profitability. Gives an investor an idea as to how efficient a company's management is at using its assets to generate earnings.

**Formula:**
```
Return on Assets = Net Profit / Total Assets

ROA (DuPont) = Net Profit Margin x Asset Turnover
             = (Net Profit / Sales) x (Sales / Assets)
```

---

### 6.5 Return on Equity (ROE)

**Definition:** Measures a company's profitability in relation to shareholders' equity. A good rule of thumb is to target an ROE **equal to or just above the average for the peer group**.

**Formula:**
```
Return on Equity = Net Profit / Average Equity

ROE (DuPont) = Net Profit Margin x Asset Turnover x Financial Leverage
             = (Net Profit / Sales) x (Sales / Assets) x (Assets / Equity)
```

---

### 6.6 Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)

**Definition:** A financial ratio used in assessing a company's profitability and capital efficiency. ROCE helps understand how well a company is generating profits from its capital as it is put to use.

Key insight from SOIC:
- The hygiene of ROCE gets reflected much more vividly in the **Balance Sheet** rather than Profit & Loss Statement.
- To a large extent, the **balance sheet is a greater reality** than what a P&L would imply.
- ROCE is a derivative of both profits (P&L) and capital employed (Balance Sheet) — so the hygiene of ROCE would more likely be evident from the balance sheet and its quality.

**Formula:**
```
ROCE = EBIT / Capital Employed

ROCE (DuPont) = (EBIT / Sales) x (Sales / Capital Employed)
              = EBIT Margin x Capital Turnover
```

**Quote — Bharat Shah:**
> "Once you understand the return on capital employed, you understand what is margin, capital turnover & other related aspects as well. When you say capital turnover, it is not only for fixed assets but also for working capital. All these data are provided in pieces and if you are able to connect them; you will be able to draw the right conclusion."

**Quote — Warren Buffett:**
> "Leaving the question of price aside, the best business to own is one that over an extended period can employ large amounts of incremental capital at very high rates of return."

---

### 6.7 Return on Incremental Invested Capital (ROIIC)

**Definition:** ROIC tells us the rate of return the company is generating on capital that has already been invested (sometimes many years ago). ROIIC goes further — it asks what return is the company generating on **new capital invested recently**.

- A company that produces high returns on capital is a good business, but what we want to know is **how much money the company can generate going forward** on future capital investments.

**Formula:**
```
ROIC = (PAT / Sales) x (Sales / (Net Fixed Assets + Net Working Capital))

ROIIC = (PAT_year1 - PAT_year0) / (Invested Capital_year1 - Invested Capital_year0)
```

---

### 6.8 Types of Moats & Their Connection to ROCE

ROCE has two components — EBIT/Sales (margin) and Sales/Capital Employed (capital turnover). Moats drive both:

**Demand-Side Moats** (drive EBIT/Sales — the margin side):
- Pricing Power
- Brand
- Network Effects
- Learning Curve
- Technical Know-How
- Niche Industries
- Switching Cost

**Supply-Side Moats** (drive Sales/Capital Employed — the capital turnover side):
- Critical Application
- Distribution
- Lowest Cost Producer
- Principal Agent

**Quote — Warren Buffett on Capital Allocation:**
> "I would say that the controlled company offers two main advantages. First, when we control a company we get to allocate capital whereas we are likely to have little or nothing to say about this process with marketable holdings. This point can be important because the heads of many companies are not skilled in capital allocation. Their inadequacy is not surprising. Most bosses rise to the top because they have excelled in an area such as marketing, production, engineering, administration or sometimes institutional politics."

## 8. Nature of Industry: How Ratios Interact

Different industries have structurally different ratio profiles. Never compare ratios across industries without context.

| Industry | Ratio Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Retail** | Higher Inventory Turnover, Lower Receivable Days, Low Margins |
| **Specialty Chemicals** | High Margins, NFAT less than 2x, High Operating Leverage |
| **Commodity Businesses** | Fluctuating Margins, Fluctuating Inventory Turns, Debtor Days fluctuate with the cycle |
| **FMCG** | Mid-teens to early 20s margins, High ROCE, Distribution reach is the key variable |
| **Internet / Platform Businesses** | Very high margins if profitable, Very high ROCE, Reinvestment Rates low (Network keeps growing), Negligible Receivable Days, No Inventory |
| **CDMO / CRAMS** | High margins if doing innovator synthesis, High Working Capital (export-oriented), Low Net Fixed Asset Turns in Pharma (1-1.5x), High in agro (2-3x), High ROCE (20%+), High Capex requirements |

## 9. How to Read an Annual Report

### What is an Annual Report?
An annual report is a **document that public corporations must provide annually to shareholders** that describes their operations and financial conditions. In India, most annual reports are released **between July-August every year**.

It consists of various highlights:
- **Future Direction** of the company
- Where the company has been in the **past**
- **Accounting policies**
- **Financial Ratios**
- **Industry analysis + Business analysis** (what shareholders need to know)

### Where to Source Annual Reports (India)
- **BSE India**: bseindia.com → Corp Announcements → Annual Reports
- **Company Website**: Investor Relations section (e.g., xyz.com/investors)
- **Screener.in**: Annual reports tab for each company
- **Tijori Finance**: tijorifinance.com → Company Filings

All are **freely available**.

---

### SOIC's Framework for Reading Annual Reports

#### BUSINESS Lens — What to Look For:
- Read and mark **future direction**
- **Industry growth** data
- **Purchase of land** (signals future capex)
- **Segmental Margins**
- **Capex plans**
- **QIP Plans** (dilution signal)
- **New product segments**
- **Business Strategy**
- **Customers** (concentration, new additions)
- **Industry trends**
- **Case Studies** in the report

#### RISK Lens — What to Look For:
- Focus on **margins** (are they sustainable?)
- **Cash flow statement** (CFO vs PAT divergence)
- **Notes to accounts** (the cheat codes)
- **Other Business Risks** section
- **Accounting** policies (changes = red flag)
- **Debt** levels and covenants
- **Miscellaneous expenses** (unusually high = dig deeper)
- **Auditor's Notes** (qualifications, emphasis of matter)

#### MANAGEMENT Lens — What to Look For:
- **Shareholder communication** quality (candid vs. promotional?)
- Whether the business is **walking the talk or not** (promised vs. delivered)
- **Remuneration of key management** (is it reasonable?)
- If remuneration is **linked to PAT** and how it moves in a down year
- **Quality of the board** (independent directors, expertise)
- Any **KMP (Key Managerial Personnel) resignations**

---

### Financial Highlights Section
Financial Highlights tell us about the **track record of the business** in terms of:
- Profitability
- Sales growth
- EBITDA growth
- Margins
- Debt

Usually the **last 5-10 years** of Financial Highlights are presented. Example: HDFC Bank 10-Year Highlights showing EPS, Return on Equity, Capital Ratios, Dividend Payout Ratio, Market Price, P/E ratio across a decade.

---

### MD, Chairman, CEO & CFO Letter
An essential part of the annual report where key management gives shareholders information on:
- **Key highlights** about the year gone by
- Potential **opportunities**
- **Weaknesses**
- **Business strategy** of the company
- Sometimes companies also mention their **growth aspirations**

**Example:** Navin Fluorine's MD letter mentioning aspirations of being a **$1 Billion company**.

---

### Management Discussion and Analysis (MD&A)
MD&A is a section of the annual report where the company's management and executives present an **analysis of the company's performance with qualitative and quantitative measures**.

Includes:
- Industry growth
- Key trends in the industry
- How the business is doing
- Various risks

**Example:** TCS MD&A showing global IT market grew 1.3x from $1,014 bn (FY2011) to $1,395 bn (FY2021), while TCS grew 2.7x from $8,187 mn to $22,174 mn — outgrowing the market.

---

### Financials: Balance Sheet, Income Statement, Cash Flow Statement
- Always **prefer to look at the Consolidated** Balance Sheet, Income Statement, and Cash Flow Statement.
- Simple reason: consolidated includes **all the subsidiaries** and represents the **true and fair picture** of the complete financials of the company.

**Example:** Hindustan Unilever — Consolidated Statement of Profit and Loss.

---

### Financials: Notes to Accounts
- Notes to accounts are like **cheat codes** — they explain each and every line item of the income statement and balance sheet **in depth**.
- Contain subsidiary details, associate companies, accounting policies, segment data.

**Example:** HUL Notes showing subsidiaries (Hindustan Unilever India Exports Ltd — 100%, Lakme Lever Private Ltd — 100%, etc.) and associates.

---

### Related Party Transactions
This part of the report explains all the business transactions carried out by the parent company and other companies it owns or has a **management overlap**.

- These transactions need to be studied properly to ensure there is **no conflict of interest**.
- Look for: loans to promoters, purchases at inflated prices, rent paid to promoter-owned entities.

**Example:** Deepak Nitrite — Related Parties Disclosures listing subsidiary companies, Key Management Personnel, and entities where KMP relatives have influence.

---

### Beginner vs. Wise Investor (Ability to Read ARs + Connect the Dots)

| Beginner | Wisdom |
|---|---|
| Focus on one industry, one type of companies, read what they are saying or doing and eventually climb up the curve | Connecting dots, understanding value chains, who is doing what, what are the barriers to entry, X-ray on the entire industry, business plans, where the business is going |

## 10. Financial Modelling

Financial Modelling = **Methods for Seeing the Full Range of Future Outcomes**

### Why is Financial Modelling Important?

**Example question:** You own an EPC stock in the Renewables Theme.
- Company Trades at 260x PE ratio
- Stock Price = Rs.260, EPS = Rs.1
- Company is guiding for 50% PAT growth over next 3 years

**Homework:** Calculate the PE ratio 3 years out, and on exit give it a 40x PE multiple — what is the expected return?

This exercise shows that financial modelling forces you to **stress-test valuations** against guidance.

---

### PE Ratio — The Foundation of Modelling

**Two ways to calculate PE ratio:**
```
Method 1: PE = Market Cap / Net Profit
Method 2: PE = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)
```

**Stock Price = PE Ratio x Earnings Per Share**

This is the **twin engines of stock returns** — returns come from either:
1. **EPS growth** (earnings growing)
2. **PE re-rating** (market willing to pay more per unit of earnings)

**Trailing Twelve Month PE (TTM PE):**
```
TTM PE = Market Cap / (EPS of last 4 quarters)

Example: Q2FY23 TTM = Q2FY23 + Q1FY23 + Q4FY22 + Q3FY22 EPS
```

**Forward PE:**
- Forward PE uses the **estimate of future PAT or Future EPS**
- One can go wrong, but it is important to have an idea
- Forward PE is what the market is actually pricing in

---

### The Samosa Shop — Understanding P&L Modelling

**Base Case:**
- Sid sells 1 lakh Samosas at Rs.5 each → Revenue = Rs.5 lakh
- Total cost = Rs.1 lakh (Rs.1 per samosa)
- PBT = Rs.4 lakh
- Tax = 25% → PAT = Rs.3 lakh

**Year 2 Forecast:**
- Sid plans to sell 2 lakh Samosas at Rs.5 each → Revenue = Rs.10 lakh
- Cost grows in line → Cost = Rs.2 lakh
- PBT = Rs.8 lakh, PAT = Rs.6 lakh

**Market Cap Exercise:**
- Samosa companies trade at 10x PE
- Base year PAT = Rs.3 lakh → Market Cap = Rs.30 lakh

**Samosa Shop Wars (Competition Scenario):**
- Sarthak & Harinder enter the market
- Selling price falls to Rs.2 (from Rs.5)
- Sid sells 1 lakh samosas, cost remains Rs.1/samosa
- New PAT = Rs.1 lakh x (Rs.2 - Rs.1) = Rs.1 lakh (pre-tax) → significantly lower
- PE also falls to 10x → Market Cap collapses

**Shubham's Dumping Scenario:**
- Shubham's area is in recession, dumps at 50 paise
- Selling price falls to Rs.0.5
- Sid's cost is still Rs.1 → Negative margins
- Profitability goes negative → Market Cap destroyed

**Key lesson:** Competition and pricing pressure are the biggest risks to any financial model. Always model the **bear case** of competition entering.

**Kumbh Mela One-Time Order:**
- Siddhant gets a bulk order of 20 lakh Samosas
- Sells at Rs.5, cost Rs.1 → PAT = Rs.60 lakh (pre-tax basis)
- At 10x PE → Market Cap = Rs.X crore
- **But next year the order doesn't repeat** and Sid sells only 1 lakh samosas again
- Market Cap collapses because earnings were **non-recurring**

**Key lesson:** Always distinguish between **recurring** and **one-time** earnings when building a model.

---

### Real Case Study: Waaree Renewables Tech
- Stock Price peaked at Rs.2,748 (April 26, 2024) with PE of **270x**
- EPS was only Rs.10.2 at the time
- Stock subsequently fell sharply as PE contracted

**Key lesson:** When a stock trades at 270x PE, even strong earnings growth may not save you from PE compression. This is why modelling the **exit PE** is as important as modelling earnings growth.

---

### Famous Company P&L — Guess the Company

A P&L was shown from Mar 2018 to Mar 2025:
- Sales growing from Rs.1,423 Cr (2018) to Rs.3,642 Cr (2025): **13% Sales CAGR (5-yr)**
- OPM steady at ~29-33%
- Net Profit: Rs.305 Cr (2018) to Rs.490 Cr (2025)
- EPS: Rs.7.64 to Rs.12.33
- **5-year Stock Price CAGR: 15%** but **3-year CAGR: 1%**, **1-year: -10%**
- ROE: 11-13% range
- Profit Growth (TTM): -9%

**Key lesson:** Good business + flat earnings = flat stock. The market caps stock returns when earnings growth stalls, even if the underlying business is high quality.

### Case Study: Syngene — Forecasting Together (FY26E Guidance)

**Management Guidance for FY26:**

- EBITDA Margin: Mid-20s (down from 28.7% in FY25 — a 220 bps drop)

**Analyst Note (Kotak Daily):**

### Bull vs. Base vs. Bear Scenario — Jubilant Pharmova

**Company Guidance (Investor Day, February 24, 2025):**
- Vision 2030: Double revenue from Rs.67,030 million (FY24) to ~Rs.1,35,000 million by FY30
- Achieve 23%-25% EBITDA margin (currently 16%)
- Turn to zero net debt (from Rs.24,570 million) by FY30
- Elevate ROCE to **high teens** from current high single-digit level

**Fundamental Drivers:**
1. **Location advantage**: India + US presence, capitalizes on geopolitical situation; Canada tariffs are an overhang but multiple manufacturing locations de-risk
2. **Improving environment in radiopharmaceuticals**: Pipeline doubled over last 5 years, ~100 radiotherapeutics under development
3. **Agile organisational structure**: Revamped recently to ensure each business segment receives adequate management bandwidth
4. **Well poised to leverage favorable market environment**: Strong balance sheet aided by Sofie divestment, resolution of Roorkee import alert, government grants
5. **Optimising to leverage assets**: Optimising product, geographic, and customer mix
6. **Cautiously investing in long-term growth**: Strategically focusing on sustainable expansion through innovation

**Bull Case:** Company achieves doubling of revenue + 25% EBITDA margins + zero net debt → ROCE reaches high teens → Re-rating warranted
**Base Case:** Company achieves 20% EBITDA margin, 1.2x-1.5x revenue growth, net debt reduces meaningfully but not zero
**Bear Case:** Radiopharma pipeline delays, Canada tariff materializes, margin expansion is slower than guided → ROCE stays in single digits → No re-rating

### Why Forecasting is Tough — Motilal Oswal Example

Motilal Oswal Financial Services P&L (Annual):
- Revenue swings wildly: Rs.769 Cr (FY15) → Rs.1,841 Cr (FY17: +74%) → Rs.2,449 Cr (FY18: +49%) → Rs.2,358 Cr (FY20: -3.7%) → Rs.3,625 Cr (FY21: +53.7%) → Rs.7,068 Cr (FY24: +69.2%)
- PAT: Rs.143 Cr (FY15) → Rs.365 Cr (FY17: +124%) → Rs.285 Cr (FY19: -54%) → Rs.215 Cr (FY20: -24.6%) → Rs.1,203 Cr (FY21: +460%) → Rs.2,446 Cr (FY24: +162%)

Quarterly data (Mar 2025 quarter): Revenue fell -44.8% YoY; PAT went to Rs.-63.2 Cr

**Key lesson:** Financial companies (brokers, AMCs, investment banks) are **extremely difficult to model** because their revenues are highly correlated to market cycles. PAT can swing +460% or -54% in a single year. For such businesses, do **scenario analysis** rather than point estimates.

### Tejas Networks — Another Forecasting Challenge

Tejas Networks P&L (2014-2025):
- Sales: Rs.423 Cr (2014) → Rs.391 Cr (FY20: -57%) → Rs.2,471 Cr (FY24: +168%) → Rs.8,923 Cr (FY25: +261%)
- PAT: Volatile — Rs.3 Cr (2014) → Rs.-237 Cr (FY20) → Rs.63 Cr (FY24) → Rs.447 Cr (FY25)
- OPM swung from -22% (FY20) to 14% (FY25)
- 10-year compounded sales growth: 37% but 5-year: 87%, 3-year: 153%

**Key lesson:** Telecom equipment companies have lumpy order execution — they can report 261% sales growth followed by potential normalization. Model the **order book, ordering cycle, and customer concentration** rather than just extrapolating recent growth.

### Drawbacks of Financial Modelling

1. Tough to forecast to exact decimal point
2. Gives a false sense of belief (overconfidence)
3. Must study the industry **first**, then create a model
4. Needs to be **updated every 6 months** (or after every major guidance change)
5. Risk reward changes with a **moving stock price** (same earnings, different price = different return)
6. Madness can last for longer than you can remain solvent (J.M. Keynes)

## Quick Reference Formula Sheet

### Activity Ratios
| Ratio | Formula |
|---|---|
| Inventory Turnover | COGS / Average Inventory |
| Inventory Days | 365 / Inventory Turnover |
| Receivable Turnover | Sales / Average Receivables |
| Receivable Days | 365 / Receivable Turnover |
| Payable Turnover | COGS / Average Payables |
| Payable Days | 365 / Payable Turnover |
| Working Capital Turnover | Sales / Average Working Capital |
| Total Asset Turnover | Sales / Average Total Assets |
| Fixed Asset Turnover | Sales / Average Fixed Assets |
| Gross Block Turnover | Sales / Gross Block |
| Cash Conversion Cycle | Inventory Days + Receivable Days - Payable Days |

### Liquidity Ratios
| Ratio | Formula |
|---|---|
| Current Ratio | Current Assets / Current Liabilities |
| Quick Ratio | (Current Assets - Inventory - Prepaid) / Current Liabilities |
| Cash Ratio | Cash & Equivalents / Current Liabilities |

### Solvency Ratios
| Ratio | Formula |
|---|---|
| Debt-Equity | (LTD + STD) / Equity |
| Interest Coverage | EBIT / Interest Expense |
| Debt-Capital | (LTD + STD) / (Total Debt + Total Equity) |
| Debt-EBITDA | Debt / EBITDA |
| Financial Leverage | Total Assets / Equity |

### Profitability & Capital Allocation Ratios
| Ratio | Formula |
|---|---|
| Gross Profit Margin | (Gross Profit / Sales) x 100 |
| Operating Profit Margin | (EBIT / Sales) x 100 |
| Net Profit Margin | (Net Profit / Sales) x 100 |
| ROA | Net Profit / Total Assets |
| ROE | Net Profit / Average Equity |
| ROCE | EBIT / Capital Employed |
| ROIIC | (PAT_y1 - PAT_y0) / (IC_y1 - IC_y0) |

### DuPont Decompositions
| Ratio | DuPont Breakdown |
|---|---|
| ROA | NPM x Asset Turnover |
| ROE | NPM x Asset Turnover x Financial Leverage |
| ROCE | EBIT Margin x Capital Turnover |

### Valuation
| Ratio | Formula |
|---|---|
| PE Ratio | Market Cap / Net Profit OR Stock Price / EPS |
| TTM PE | Market Cap / (Sum of last 4 quarters EPS) |
| Stock Price | PE Ratio x EPS |
