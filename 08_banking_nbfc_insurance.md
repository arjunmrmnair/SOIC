## 2. The "Right to Win" Framework

### For Banks
- **Raw material (Liabilities):** Low-cost, granular CASA — best CASA comes from frequently transacting customers, not just account holders
- **Product portfolio:** Wide basket (lending + payments + investing) ensures stickiness; more use cases = more transactions = more fee income = platform effect
- **Customer engagement:** Fee income drives very high RoE; fee income as % of net income signals pricing power and customer lock-in
- **Risk management:** Macro and micro risk management (leadership + processes) — the bank must control what can be controlled at the individual loan level, not just macro cycles

### For NBFCs
- **Raw material (Liabilities):** Trust from bond market and retail investors signals good lending practices; "parentage" (who owns the NBFC) is critical for funding access
- **Product portfolio:** Deep understanding of a niche customer segment (e.g., CV financing, gold, affordable housing)
- **Customer engagement:** Happy customers return to borrow again — repeat borrowers reduce search costs and opex
- **Risk management:** Systems and processes for credit assessment and collections; technology advantage in underwriting

### Common Outcome
**Good growth + Good profits = High RoA**

## 3. Key Banking Metrics with Thresholds

| Metric | Good | Average | Poor | Description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **NIM (Net Interest Margin)** | >3.5% | 2.5-3.5% | <2.5% | Like gross profit margin for a bank. Higher NIMs come from unsecured lending; large banks can earn good NIMs even in secured due to low cost of funds |
| **GNPA%** | <2% | 2-5% | >5% | Gross Non-Performing Assets: loans where borrower hasn't paid for 90+ days. Watch for rapid deterioration quarter-on-quarter |
| **NNPA%** | <1% | 1-3% | >3% | Net NPA = GNPA minus provisions. Shows actual net risk on books after accounting for money set aside |
| **PCR (Provision Coverage Ratio)** | >70% | 50-70% | <50% | PCR = Provisions/GNPAs. Shows how much of bad loans the bank has already provisioned. Highest ever PCR signals clean-up is complete |
| **CASA Ratio** | >40% | 30-40% | <30% | Current Account + Savings Account as % of total deposits. Low-cost, sticky deposits. Best CASA = from frequently transacting customers |
| **CAR Tier 1** | >12% | 10-12% | <10% | Capital Adequacy Ratio — buffer against losses. Required regulatory minimum; higher is safer |
| **ROA** | >1.5% | 1-1.5% | <0.7% | Return on Assets. For large private banks: 1.8%+. For PSBs: 0.0-0.7%. For SFBs/NBFCs: can be 2-8% |
| **ROE** | >15% | 10-15% | <10% | Return on Equity. At 20%+ ROE and 10x leverage, the book value compounds dramatically over time |
| **Cost-to-Income** | <50% | 50-60% | >60% | Total opex / Total income. Falling C/I ratio as income grows = operating leverage kicking in |
| **Slippage Ratio** | <1% | 1-2% | >2% | Fresh NPAs / Standard Advances at beginning of period. Key leading indicator for asset quality deterioration |
| **Credit Cost** | <1% | 1-2% | >2% | Provisioning / Average AUM. Cyclical: spikes in bad times, falls in good times. Watch this to time PSB investments |
| **Loan CAGR** | >15% | 10-15% | <10% | Growth of loan book. A smaller bank growing slower than large private banks is a red flag |

### Additional Asset Quality KPIs
- **SMA (Special Mention Accounts):** SMA-0 = overdue 1-30 days; SMA-1 = 31-60 days; SMA-2 = 61-90 days. Rising SMA buckets are the earliest warning of stress before NPA classification
- **Restructured Book:** Loans whose terms have been renegotiated. High restructuring = potential future NPA. Watch this during and after macro stress events (e.g., COVID)
- **Stressed Loans:** GNPA + SMA-2 + Restructured book = total stress in system
- **Write-offs:** Banks write off loans they can no longer recover. Net NPA can look artificially clean if write-offs are aggressive

## 4. How to Read a Bank's Balance Sheet and P&L

### Income Statement Structure
```
Interest Income (on loans, investments)
- Interest Expended (cost of deposits, borrowings)
= Net Interest Income (NII)

+ Other Income:
    - Fee income (loan processing, third-party sales like insurance)
    - Treasury income (gains/losses on bond portfolio)
    - Dividends from subsidiaries

= Total Income (NII + Other Income)

- Operating Expenses (Staff + Branch + Technology + Power)
= Pre-Provisioning Operating Profit (PPOP)

- Credit Cost (Provisioning for bad loans)
= PBT (Profit Before Tax)
- Tax
= PAT (Profit After Tax)
```

### Key Relationships
- **NIM** = Net Interest Income / Average Interest-Earning Assets — like the gross margin
- **Spreads** = Gross Advance Yield minus Cost of Funds — what the bank earns per rupee lent vs what it pays per rupee borrowed
- **Treasury Income** moves inversely with interest rates: rates up = bond prices down = treasury losses; rates down = treasury gains
- **PPOP / Assets** = the bank's operating efficiency before credit costs; rising PPOP while credit costs fall = massive PAT growth (non-linear)
- **Cost to Assets** = cost as % of assets; if this falls as AUM grows, the bank is achieving true operating leverage

### Balance Sheet
**Liabilities (Sources of Funds):**
- CASA (sticky, low-cost)
- Term Deposits (retail)
- Wholesale/Bulk Deposits (non-sticky, costly)
- Borrowings (bonds, etc.)

**Assets (Uses of Funds):**
- Loans and Advances (the core earning asset)
- Investments (SLR — Statutory Liquidity Ratio mandatory holdings)
- CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio — held with RBI, earns nothing)
- Fixed Assets, Other Assets

**Key Insight:** Banks operate at 8-10x leverage (equity). A 9x leveraged bank with 10% ROE grows book value at 10% per year. At 20% ROE, book value compounds at 20% per year — making ROE the single most important metric.

## 5. What Creates Value vs Destroys Value in Banks

### Value Creation Levers
1. **Change in product mix:** Shift from lower-margin products (secured corporate) to higher-margin products (retail, unsecured). Leads to NIM expansion
2. **Improving asset quality:** Falling NPAs + lower slippages + write-backs = rapidly falling credit costs. PAT grows non-linearly because opex is fixed but provisioning falls
3. **Operating leverage:** Once branches, staff, and technology are built, incremental income flows mostly to profit. Falling C/I ratio is the signature of this
4. **Retail/granular shift:** Moving from corporate loans to retail loans reduces concentration risk; moving from wholesale deposits to retail deposits reduces funding volatility
5. **ROE crossing 15-20%:** Triggers re-rating; ROA crossing 1.8% for large banks is the threshold for premium valuation
6. **Subsidiary value unlock:** Life/general insurance, asset management, securities — often worth Rs 100-600/share for large banks (HDFC: Rs 100/share from HDB Financial; ICICI: Rs 200/share from subsidiaries; Kotak: Rs 600/share from Life, Securities, AMC)

### Value Destruction Red Flags
1. **Aggressive growth in good times:** Banks that grow recklessly during boom years always pay in bad years — leveraged entities cannot absorb credit shocks
2. **Poor jockey:** Management change, RBI-appointed board members (a regulatory red flag), divergence notices from RBI (bank understated NPAs vs RBI assessment)
3. **Divergence from RBI:** When RBI's assessment of NPAs differs significantly from the bank's own reported numbers — a major red flag signaling accounting manipulation
4. **CEO resignation or sudden change:** Especially if unexplained or regulatory-linked
5. **Sudden provisioning spikes:** One-quarter exceptional provisions often signal problems that have been hidden; management that discovers problems "suddenly" is suspicious
6. **Sub-15% AUM growth for smaller banks:** A Rs 50,000 crore bank growing at 8-10% when HDFC Bank at 10x the size grows at 15% signals loss of competitive position
7. **Highest restructured loans in segment (RSL loans):** DCB Bank's weakness — having the highest RSL exposure vs peers
8. **Non-investment in products:** Staying in 1-2 products limits eventual growth ceiling (City Union Bank = MSME-only; cannot compete with large banks on fee income or cross-sell)
9. **Poor ROA/ROE despite retail claims:** Banks claiming to be "retail-focused" but still reporting 0.3-1.2% ROA are not translating strategy into results

### Case Studies in Value Destruction
- **DCB Bank:** AUM growth sub-15%, highest RSL loans, poor ROA/ROE despite smaller size — grew slower than HDFC Bank even though much smaller
- **City Union Bank:** Did not invest in areas beyond MSME loans; poor growth rates 10-12%; higher restructuring; RBI divergence in asset quality
- **Axis Bank (historical):** Poor ROA/ROE despite claiming to be a retail bank — higher credit costs due to poor lending in wholesale loans
- **RBL Bank:** Asset quality issues, spike in credit costs, GNPA spike

## 6. The ROE Compounding Engine — Why ROE is Everything

**HDFC Bank ROE History (FY96-FY22):** Consistently 16-29% ROE for 26+ years. This is why the stock has been an extraordinary compounder. Book value went from Rs 1.22 (FY96) to Rs 432.95 (FY22).

### Mathematical Power of High ROE
**Scenario 1: 10% ROE, 9x leverage**
- Year 1: Equity Rs 100, Loans Rs 1,000, Profit Rs 10
- Year 2: Equity Rs 110, Loans Rs 1,100, Profit Rs 11

**Scenario 2: 20% ROE, 9x leverage**
- Year 1: Equity Rs 100, Loans Rs 1,000, Profit Rs 20
- Year 2: Equity Rs 120, Loans Rs 1,200, Profit Rs 24

**Implication:** The 20% ROE bank grows book value 2x faster and its earnings compound at 20% vs 10%. Over 10+ years, the divergence is enormous. This is why great banks trade at high P/BV multiples.

**ROE = ROA x Leverage.** For a bank at 9x leverage:
- ROA 1% → ROE ~9%
- ROA 1.5% → ROE ~13-15%
- ROA 1.8-2% → ROE ~16-20%
- ROA 3%+ (small NBFCs) → ROE 25%+ at lower leverage

## 7. Asset Quality — Red Flags and NPA Classification

### NPA Classification (RBI Rules)
- **Standard Account:** Current, no default
- **SMA-0:** Overdue 1-30 days (earliest warning)
- **SMA-1:** Overdue 31-60 days
- **SMA-2:** Overdue 61-90 days (watch carefully — banks must disclose)
- **Sub-standard NPA:** Classified NPA for <12 months
- **Doubtful NPA:** NPA for 1-3 years
- **Loss Asset:** NPA >3 years or identified as uncollectible

### Key Early Warning Signals
- Rising SMA buckets quarter-on-quarter, especially SMA-2
- Increasing restructured book as % of loans
- Divergence between bank-reported NPAs and RBI assessment
- Slippage ratio increasing beyond 2%
- Sudden jump in credit costs without prior warning
- Provisioning increasing without corresponding NPA increase (building buffer)
- CEO/senior management exits, especially in credit function
- Sudden withdrawal from certain geographies or segments

### Stressed Asset Framework
Total Stress = GNPA + SMA-2 + Restructured Book + Written-Off loans
- Banks often look cleaner than reality if write-offs are aggressive
- PCR rising while GNPA falls = cleanup in progress; PCR falling = potentially hiding stress

### Provision Coverage Ratio (PCR)
- PCR >70%: Comfortable — if loans go bad, most already provisioned
- PCR < 50%: Risky — further losses will hit capital directly
- Axis Bank achieving "highest ever PCR with lowest NNPA" was the key signal that legacy issues were cleaned up

## 8. Loan Book Quality Assessment by Segment

### Risk by Loan Type (Lower to Higher Risk)
1. **Home Loans (salaried, LTV <80%):** Lowest risk — collateral + sticky income
2. **LAP (Loan Against Property):** Moderate; builder/commercial LAP is riskier
3. **Gold Loans:** Very low NPA if pure gold book; risk rises with non-gold additions
4. **Vehicle Finance (PV/4-Wheeler):** Moderate; CV is cyclical
5. **MSME/SME:** Moderate-high; sensitive to economic cycles
6. **Corporate Loans:** Can be very low or very high depending on credit quality; infra/power = high risk historically
7. **Microfinance (MFI/JLG):** High yields, cyclical; crises emerge from over-leverage of borrowers
8. **Personal Loans/Unsecured Consumer:** Highest risk (and highest yield); very volatile credit costs

### Key Signals by Segment
- **Housing Finance:** NPA in builder/LAP portfolio is key red flag. LIC Housing's 19% of book in LAP/builder = weakness
- **Vehicle Finance (CV):** Cyclical with auto sector. Shriram Transport's strength = deep lend-and-collect model
- **Gold Loans:** Muthoot's virtuous cycle = low loan rate → high per-branch AUM → low opex. Manappuram's weakness = non-gold book at 33%
- **MFI:** Crises emerge from political interference (AP crisis), borrower over-leverage, and monsoon failures. CreditAccess = rural focus with good business controls
- **Corporate Loans:** PSBs historically concentrated here; private banks now moving toward retail to reduce volatility

## 9. Credit Cost Cycle

Credit costs are inherently cyclical and drive most of the variance in bank profitability:

```
GOOD CYCLE (low credit costs):
Low NPAs → Low provisioning → High PPOP flows to PAT → High ROE → Premium valuation

BAD CYCLE (high credit costs):
NPAs rise → Provisioning spikes → PPOP absorbed by provisions → Low/negative PAT → Discount valuation
```

### When to Buy PSBs
PSBs (SBI, BoB, Canara) have the highest credit cost volatility. Buy them when:
- GNPA is at cycle peak (everyone knows it's bad)
- Credit costs are beginning to decline
- ROA is starting to improve from 0% toward 0.5-0.7%
- NIM is stable or improving
- Valuations are cheap (P/BV < 1x)

**Bank of Baroda example:** Turning around with 18% ROE, diversified retail + corporate mix, credit cost targeting <1%, NIMs sustainable.

### What Makes Credit Costs Non-Linear?
1. Provisioning accumulates from P&L (reduces PAT)
2. When NPAs stabilize, provisioning falls
3. When NPAs fall, write-backs occur (reversals boost PAT)
4. PAT growth is therefore non-linear: slow on the way up, explosive on the way down

**IDFC First Bank turnaround logic:** If cost-to-income falls from 70%+ to 60%, AND cost of funds falls as legacy borrowings are replaced, AND credit costs normalize — all three happen simultaneously. PAT math becomes compelling very quickly.

## 10. NBFC-Specific Metrics vs Banks

### NBFCs Cannot Take Deposits (Key Difference)
- Must borrow from banks, bond markets, and retail NCDs
- **Liability profile is everything** — parentage (who owns the NBFC) determines funding cost and stability
- Trust from bond market = signal of sound lending practices
- Parentage quality ranking: Strong (Chola/HDFC parentage), Medium (Shriram), Weak (standalone/promoter-backed)

### NBFC-Specific Metrics
| Metric | Description | What to Watch |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost of Funds (CoF)** | All-in borrowing cost | Competitive advantage: Muthoot at 7.5% vs Manappuram at 8.1% |
| **Spread** | Yield on advances minus CoF | NBFCs typically earn higher spreads (3-8%) vs banks (2-4%) |
| **AUM / Branch** | Revenue productivity per branch | Muthoot: high AUM per branch = low opex per unit |
| **Collection Efficiency** | % of dues collected | Critical for MFI and vehicle finance; falls in stress |
| **Asset-Liability Mismatch (ALM)** | Duration gap between assets and liabilities | NBFCs with long-term assets funded by short-term borrowings face rollover risk |
| **Granularity of funding** | Number of lenders/investors | Concentrated funding = vulnerability during market stress |

### NBFC Categories and Key Distinctions
- **Housing Finance (HFC):** Lower risk if salaried-focused, higher NIM than banks, but funding costs higher. Affordable HFCs (Aavas, Aptus, HomeFirst) = higher RoA (2.5-8%) vs traditional HFCs (0.8-2%)
- **Vehicle Finance:** Cyclical; best operators have deep lend-and-collect capabilities and diversified vehicle mix
- **Gold Loans:** Almost no NPA risk (collateral secured); pure gold companies (Muthoot) vs diversified (Manappuram with 33% non-gold)
- **MFI:** High yields (20%+) but volatile credit costs; unsecured, cyclical; best in rural areas with good control systems
- **Multi-product (Bajaj Finance):** The NBFC that has successfully built a bank-like franchise without a banking license — fee income, cross-sell, very diversified

## 19. Valuation Framework — Banks and NBFCs

### P/Adjusted Book Value (P/Adj B) — Primary Method for Banks
- P/Adj B = Market Price / (Book Value adjusted for NNPAs and intangibles)
- **Premium valuation (3x+ Adj B):** ROE >18%, loan CAGR >20%, pristine asset quality, expanding fee income (HDFC, Kotak, AU SFB, Chola)
- **Fair valuation (1.5-3x Adj B):** ROE 13-18%, growing above 15%, improving trajectory (ICICI, Muthoot)
- **Discount valuation (<1x Adj B):** ROE <12%, volatile credit costs, PSBs in bad cycle, structural challenges (PNB Housing, Repco, KVB)

### What Drives P/Adj B Multiple
The P/Adj B multiple is a function of:
1. Sustainable ROE (higher ROE = higher multiple)
2. Growth (higher loan CAGR = higher multiple)
3. Asset quality confidence (lower uncertainty = higher multiple)
4. Franchise quality (deposits, fee income, cross-sell = higher multiple)
5. Cyclical position (improving cycle = re-rating; worsening = de-rating)

### Valuation for NBFCs
- Same P/BV methodology; premium for high RoA + growth + niche moat
- Affordable HFCs and specialty NBFCs can command 4-6x BV at peak
- Gold loan companies typically 1.5-2.5x BV at fair value

### When to Buy Banks
- PSBs: When GNPA is at cycle peak; credit costs starting to fall; ROA improving from 0 to 0.5%; P/BV < 1x
- Private banks in stress: When asset quality deterioration is known, PCR is high, and management credibility is being re-established
- SFBS/NBFCs in turnaround: When operating leverage is beginning to kick in (C/I falling, credit costs normalizing)

## 20. General Insurance — How the Industry Works

### Core Concept
Insurance is a financial tool to reduce the financial impact of unforeseen events by pooling risk across many policyholders. 1,000 families each contribute a small amount (Rs 5K) to create a common pool of Rs 50L, which covers whoever actually needs Rs 50L for healthcare.

### Types
- **Life Insurance:** Death protection, savings, endowment, pension
- **Non-Life/General Insurance:** Accident, fire, health, vehicle, crop, property damage — everything except death
- **Reinsurance:** Insurance for insurers — the insurer (cedant) passes risk to reinsurer for a premium

### Indian Insurance Market Structure
- Regulator: IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India)
- 24 Life Insurance players (1 public = LIC, 23 private)
- 21 General Insurance players (4 public, 17 private)
- 6 Standalone Health Insurers (SAHIs)
- 2 Re-insurers
- **Megatrend:** Private sector market share grew from 2% (FY03) to 29.6% (FY16) in life; 7% (FY02) to 61% (FY20) in general. This shift from PSU to private is still continuing

### De-Tariffication History
- Pre-2008: Premiums for motor, fire, engineering were regulated by Tariff Advisory Committee
- Post-Jan 2008: Free pricing regime; premiums actuarially determined
- Only Motor Third Party (TP) remains tariffed (mandatory, regulated premium)

### Industry Size and Growth
- Non-life insurance CAGR: Phase I (FY02-07) = 17.2%; Phase II (FY08-12) = 18.5%; Phase III (FY13-20) = 15.5%
- India's non-life penetration: 1.0% of GDP (FY19) vs world average of ~2.8% — massive underpenetration
- Industry projected to grow at 14.8% CAGR FY20-30; GDPI to reach Rs 7.5 trillion by FY30

## 21. Insurance Metrics — Key KPIs and Definitions

### Premium Flow
```
Gross Written Premium (GWP)
= All premiums received in the year (new + renewal business)
= New Business Premium + Renewal Premium

Net Written Premium (NWP) = GWP - Reinsurance Ceded

Net Premium Earned (NPE) = Share of NWP attributed to current year
(remainder is Unearned Premium Reserve for future years)

Ceded Ratio = Reinsurance Premium / GWP (what % passed to reinsurer)
Retention Ratio = 1 - Ceded Ratio (what % kept by insurer)
```

### Profitability Ratios
| Metric | Formula | Good | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Loss Ratio** | Claims Incurred / NPE | <70% for retail health; varies | Core underwriting quality |
| **Commission Ratio** | Commissions Paid / NWP | <15% | Distribution cost efficiency |
| **Expense Ratio** | All G&A expenses / NWP (excl. commission) | <30% | Operating efficiency |
| **Combined Ratio (COR)** | Loss Ratio + Expense Ratio + Commission Ratio | <100% | <100% = underwriting profit; >100% = underwriting loss |
| **Solvency Ratio** | Available Solvency Margin / Required Solvency Margin | >1.5x (regulatory minimum); good >2.0x | Financial strength; IRDAI intervenes below 1.5x |
| **Investment Yield** | Investment Income / Average Investments | 7-10% | Crucial for profitability when combined ratio >100% |
| **Claims Settlement Ratio** | Claims settled / Claims received | Higher is better | Customer promise; key for brand trust |
| **Grievance Ratio** | Grievances per 10,000 policies | Lower is better | Customer satisfaction |

### Float — The Hidden Profit Engine
- Insurance companies collect premiums before paying claims (lag of months to years)
- This "float" is invested to earn returns
- Even with combined ratio >100% (underwriting loss), if investment return on float is high enough, total profitability can be positive
- **Berkshire Hathaway's secret:** Insurance float invested in equities over decades
- **Indian reality:** Private insurer COR has mostly remained above 100%; profit comes from float
- **Key insight:** Growing float + decent investment return + managing COR close to 100% = sustainable profitable business

### Reserving Triangle
- For long-tail business (Motor TP), claims take years to settle
- Insurers must estimate IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported) claims
- Reserving Triangle = a matrix showing how reserves evolve over time
- Red flag: If reserves consistently prove insufficient (releases are negative), the insurer is under-reserving
- ICICI Lombard was only Indian non-life company disclosing reserving triangles publicly for many years (good transparency signal)

## 22. General Insurance — Business Levers and Profitability

### How Insurance Companies Make Money
```
UNDERWRITING INCOME:
(1 - Combined Ratio) x NWP
= Underwriting Profit/Loss

+ INVESTMENT INCOME:
Investment Amount x Rate of Return

= TOTAL INCOME / PROFIT before tax
```

### Key Value Drivers (KPI → Driver)
| KPI | Business Driver |
|---|---|
| GWP (Topline) | GDP growth + Pricing cycle + Inflation + Competition |
| Combined Ratio | Claims severity + Claims frequency + Cost management + Pricing cycle |
| Reserving Quality | Reserving triangle disclosure; underwriting quality; actuarial conservatism |
| Investment Yield | Asset allocation (equity vs debt) + Interest rate cycle + Asset quality |

### Management Quality Signals
- Management that can keep combined ratio low despite respectable growth
- Grows float without major surprises (no sudden reserve strengthening)
- Treats minority shareholders well; transparent disclosures
- Conservative: willing to sacrifice growth to maintain balance sheet quality

### Global Learnings
- Growth is not linear — driven by competition, macro-economics, weather events
- Reversion to mean is reality: combined ratios spike and then normalize
- At peak competitive cycle, most management mistakes occur
- Long-tail (Motor TP) business can have surprise losses appearing years later

## 23. Motor Insurance Segment

### Market Structure
- Largest segment: 36% of industry GWP (FY20)
- Split: Motor OD (Own Damage) + Motor TP (Third Party)
- Motor TP is mandatory by law (Motor Vehicles Act 1988)
- Motor TP is tariffed = regulated price; Motor OD is market-priced

### Key Dynamics
- TP business: long-tail (3/5-year product), high float, mandatory growth driver
- OD business: short-tail, price-competitive, growing slowly as new vehicle sales slow
- ~52% of vehicles do not renew after year 1 (especially 2-wheelers) → large untapped market
- New Motor Vehicles Act 2019: reduced time limit for TP claims to 6 months (previously no limit → massive fraud reduction)
- ~65-70% of TP claims were filed after 6 months previously = fraudulent claims

### Competitive Landscape
- ICICI Lombard: leader in both OD and TP
- Private sector took OD share from 44% → 72% (FY20)
- New-age players (Go Digit, Acko) aggressively gaining share using digital channels
- Risk: Motor TP de-tariffication could lead to extreme price competition; reserve triangle disclosure critical for TP

### Claims Ratios by Product (ICICI Lombard FY2021)
- Motor OD: 62.2% (good)
- Motor TP: 69.7% (improving, was 107% in FY2018)
- Health: 78.0%
- Crop: 111.2% (loss-making)
- Fire: 63.7%

## 24. Health Insurance Segment

### Market Structure
- Second-largest segment: 27% of GWP
- Three types of players: Private multi-line insurers, Standalone Health Insurers (SAHIs), PSU insurers
- SAHIs (Star Health, CARE Health, Max Bupa, HDFC Ergo Health, ManipalCigna, Aditya Birla Health): specialize only in health
- SAHIs have 46% market share in retail health (FY21)
- Three customer types: Retail (individual/family), Group (corporate), Government (RSBY, PM-JAY, CGHS)

### Why SAHIs Outperform
- Retail focus: SAHIs have 76% retail vs 29% for private multi-line and 19% for PSUs
- Retail = most profitable (lowest claims ratio: 63-72%) vs Group (120%+) and Government schemes
- Single product specialization = deeper underwriting expertise
- Agents don't need additional training = easier to grow agent network
- SAHIs have grown at 2x industry average in retail

### Growth Drivers
- Health GWP: 20% CAGR FY10-20
- India's healthcare spending is rising; COVID-19 created permanent awareness shift
- Premium per life increasing due to better pricing and higher sum assured
- Pricing can now be increased annually (vs earlier 3-year lock)
- Only 1 in 3 Indians have any health insurance

### Key Risks for Health Insurers
1. Mass health infection risk (COVID-type events)
2. Competitive and pricing risk (too many players, group business has corporate bargaining power)
3. Regulatory risk (IRDAI considering allowing life insurers to sell indemnity health products)
4. Disruption risk (tech platforms, Telemedicine)
5. Medical inflation driving claims higher (hospitals gaming the system in cashless segment)

### Performance Benchmarks (Health Segment)
- Retail claims ratio: 71-72% (good)
- Group claims ratio: 100-125% (loss-making in isolation)
- PSU claims ratio: 100-102% (break-even at best)
- Industry combined ratio: ~117% — profit comes almost entirely from investment income on float

## 25. Insurance Company Balance Sheet and P&L

### Revenue Account (Policyholders' Account / Technical Account)
```
Premiums Earned (Net) — NPE
+ Fees and Commission Income
+ Investment Income from Terror Pool
+ Interest, Dividend and Rent
+ Other Income
= Total Income (A)

- Claims Incurred (Net)
- Commission (Net)
- Operating Expenses
= Operating Profit / Loss (A - B)
```

### P&L Account (Shareholders' Account)
```
Operating Profit from Technical Account
+ Investment Income (Shareholders' Fund)
+ Other Income
= Total Income

- Expenses (non-insurance)
- Depreciation
= Profit Before Tax
- Tax
= Profit After Tax
```

### Balance Sheet
- **Sources of Funds:** Share Capital + Reserves + Borrowings
- **Investments - Policyholders:** Money backing policyholder liabilities — Reserves for outstanding claims, IBNR, URR (Unearned Risk Reserve)
- **Investments - Shareholders:** Free float of shareholders
- **Current Liabilities:** Unearned premium, advance premium
- **Provision:** For known claims, IBNR

**Key insight:** Insurance balance sheets are complex. The policyholders' fund (invested) is NOT the company's money — it backs future claims. The shareholders' fund is the real net worth. Provisions must be adequate; understated provisions inflate current profitability at cost of future losses.

## 29. Overall Mental Models and Dos/Don'ts

### For Banks and NBFCs

**MUST-DO:**
- Always look at the full cycle ROA and ROE, not just peak year
- Check if GNPA, NNPA, slippage trends are improving or worsening
- Understand the funding profile (CASA %, cost of funds, liability granularity)
- Compare with peers — a smaller bank growing slower than larger banks is a structural weakness
- Understand the product mix — secured vs unsecured, corporate vs retail
- Watch SMA buckets before official NPA numbers change
- Look for operating leverage: C/I falling as income grows
- Check subsidiary values for large banks (often 10-30% of market cap embedded)
- Evaluate management's track record across cycles — did they maintain discipline when growth was easy?

**RED FLAGS (AVOID):**
- RBI divergence or RBI-appointed board members
- Sudden unexplained CEO exits
- Consistent restructured book as % of loans rising quarter-on-quarter
- Claims to be "retail bank" but with ROA <1%
- Single-segment concentration (e.g., all MFI, all CV) without diversification plans
- Aggressive growth without commensurate capital buildup
- Below-peer loan CAGR for a small bank

**VALUATION RED FLAGS:**
- Paying >3x P/BV for a bank with ROE below 15%
- Paying >2x P/BV for a NBFC with volatile/declining ROA
- Ignoring NPL trends because valuation "looks cheap"

### For General Insurance

**MUST-DO:**
- Focus on combined ratio trend — below 100 is good, below 95 is excellent
- Demand reserving triangle disclosures (at least 7-8 years of history)
- Understand the product mix: retail is best, group is breakeven, government is loss-making
- Look for float growth over time — a growing float is the business engine
- Check solvency ratio (>2x is good, below 1.5x is regulatory intervention territory)
- Evaluate management conservatism — prefer those willing to sacrifice growth for balance sheet quality
- Track investment portfolio quality (exposure to Yes Bank, DHFL type bonds was damaging)

**RED FLAGS:**
- Consistently aggressive reserving (reserves too thin; profits look great now but future claims hidden)
- High concentration in unprofitable segments (crop, government health)
- PSU solvency below regulatory minimum
- Management claims combined ratio will improve without structural changes

**SUMMARY INSIGHT (General Insurance):**
- The Combined Ratio is the "Brahmastra" — it tells you everything about underwriting discipline
- The Reserving Triangle is the "secret sauce" — without it, you can't trust the numbers
- Managing float with decent return while growing it without big surprises = the formula for long-term compounding
- Private sector will continue to gain market share from PSU insurers

### Warren Buffett Principle for Banking
> "Banking is very good business if you don't do anything dumb."

The history of Indian banking is full of banks that did dumb things (aggressive corporate lending without assessment, growth without provisioning, hiding NPAs). The banks that consistently maintained discipline (HDFC Bank) compounded at 20%+ ROE for 26 consecutive years. The lesson: in banking, avoiding stupidity is more important than finding genius.
