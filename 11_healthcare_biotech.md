| HealthCare Global (HCG) | 32,632 |
| Narayana Hrudayalaya | 28,493 |
| KIMS | 20,609 |

**Metro vs Tier-2 ARPOB Benchmarks**
- Delhi NCR: INR 50,000/day
- Mumbai: INR 45,000-46,000/day
- Chennai: INR 42,000/day
- Bengaluru: INR 38,000/day
- Hyderabad: INR 33,000/day
- KIMS (Hyderabad/AP focused): ~Rs. 26,000/day (H1FY22)
- Narayana Hrudayalaya: ~Rs. 32,000/day (lower due to value focus and South India mix)

**Levers to improve ARPOB:**
1. Better payor mix (less institutional, more cash/TPA)
2. Complex therapy mix (organ transplants, robotics, oncology)
3. Lower ALOS (more efficient throughput)
4. Hiring senior clinicians for complex therapies

**Definition:** Occupied Beds / Total Available Beds x 100

**Mature hospital benchmark:** 65-75% occupancy is considered good/healthy.
**Ramp-up phase (greenfield/new beds):** Occupancy can be as low as 30-45% in initial years.

**Actual occupancy trends (H1FY22)**
- Max Healthcare: 78% (highest — metro concentration helps)
- KIMS: 72%
- Fortis: 65%
- Apollo: 66%

- HCG (oncology focus): 43-55%
- Sunshine Hospital (before KIMS acquisition): ~40%

**Key insight:** HCG's low occupancy (43%) despite being a pure oncology play reflects the capital-intensive ramp-up and that cancer patients often use day-care beds (chemo) which reduces reported inpatient occupancy.

### 1.5 Mature vs Greenfield Beds — Evaluation Framework

**The core challenge in hospital chain analysis:** A chain with many new/greenfield beds will show depressed overall EBITDA margins, high capex, and low occupancy. You must separate mature bed economics from ramp-up beds.

**Mature bed indicators:**
- Occupancy >65%
- EBITDA margins 25-32%
- ALOS at specialty norms
- Minimal incremental capex requirement

**Greenfield/ramp-up bed characteristics:**
- Takes 3-5 years to reach mature occupancy
- Year 1-2: EBITDA margins near zero or negative
- High upfront capex (Rs. 5-10M+ per bed)
- Revenue ramp follows as doctors build referral networks and brand is established

**How to evaluate:**
- Always disaggregate mature bed EBITDA from overall reported numbers
- Track quarterly occupancy of new bed cohorts
- Compare ARPOB of new vs mature units (new beds often have ARPOB discount)
- Watch ROCE trajectory — should improve from 2-5% (ramp-up) to 15-25% (mature)

**KIMS strategy:** Contiguous regional expansion (Andhra Pradesh + Telangana first), then adjacent states. Low-cost Tier-2 acquisitions with doctor equity partnership model — aligns doctor interests.

**KIMS doctor equity model:** Doctors at acquired hospitals given 20-49% equity in local subsidiaries, significantly reducing doctor attrition risk.

**Asset-light strategy (Max example):**
- O&M arrangements: Partner/REIT builds and owns, Max operates and manages
- Revenue share model with minimum guarantee
- Reduces capex burden, improves ROCE
- Max plans ~1,000 beds each via asset-light O&M and greenfield

### 1.6 Revenue Mix

**In-patient vs Out-patient split:** IP: 76% revenue, OP: 24%

**In-patient revenue components:** Rentals, Consultancy, Diagnostics, Consumables, Surgery costs
**Out-patient revenue components:** Consultancy, Day surgery, Diagnostics

**Payor mix (critical for ARPOB):**
- Cash/Self-pay: Highest ARPOB
- TPA (insurance): ~80-90% of cash ARPOB
- Corporate: Similar to TPA
- Government/Institutional (Aarogyasree, CGHS, ESI): ~40% lower ARPOB than cash
- International: Highest-value but volume dependent on medical tourism

**KIMS payor mix (Q2FY22, optimal):** Cash 60.2%, Insurance 18.1%, Corporate 11.4%, Aarogyasree 10.2%
**Narayana payor mix:** Government schemes 23%, Insured 24%, Walk-in 52%, International 1%

**Key insight:** Narayana's 23% government/scheme mix explains its lower ARPOB versus Max despite similar-quality clinical outcomes. This is the core reason for ARPOB divergence between hospitals.

---

### 1.7 Red Flags

1. **High receivable days from government/institutional business:** Government schemes (Aarogyasree, PMJAY) pay slowly. High institutional mix leads to working capital strain and receivable days >90 days being a red flag.

2. **High capex without ramp-up:** Continuous high capex spending (>Rs. 500Cr/year for a mid-size chain) without proportional bed addition or occupancy improvement signals management's inability to allocate capital efficiently. Track ROCE: if capex is high but ROCE stays below 10%, something is wrong (Aster DM CAPEX/ROCE example shows ROCE declined from 15% to 10% despite Rs. 520-578Cr annual capex FY18-19).

3. **Greenfield in new city without contiguous strategy:** Entering an unfamiliar geography without established referral networks, doctor relationships, or brand recognition destroys value. Apollo's 2013-2019 expansion to multiple greenfield hospitals in diverse geographies led to ROCE declining from 13% to 7%.

4. **Over-reliance on government schemes in Tier-2:** ARPOB stays depressed and receivables balloon.

5. **Physician concentration risk:** If top 5 doctors drive 40%+ of revenue, their departure is existential. KIMS's doctor-equity model mitigates this.

6. **Low occupancy (below 50%) at so-called 'mature' hospitals:** Indicates either ineffective marketing, poor location, or misaligned specialty mix.

## PART 2: BIOTECHNOLOGY — UNSEEN TRENDS

Speaker: Sajal Kapoor (@unseenvalue), SOIC Webinar

### 2.1 The Big Picture: Biotechnology as Industry 5.0

Biotechnology is the defining technology of Industry 5.0 (Vision 2): "Bioeconomy" with sustainability as the motto. Unlike Industry 4.0 (IoT, AI, Robotics) or Industry 5.0 Vision 1 (Human-Robot collaboration), Vision 2 is about biology-driven sustainable production across agriculture, energy, textiles, and medicine.

**Indian Bioeconomy target:** $100-150 Billion by 2025.

**Biotech impact will be multi-sector and multi-decadal:**
- Human & Animal Health: Vaccines, therapeutics, biologics, mRNA, gene therapy
- Agriculture: CRISPR crop editing, biopesticides, biofertilizers
- Industrial: Fermentation, enzymes, biocatalysis, textiles, detergents
- Energy: Bio-ethanol, bio-refineries
- Diagnostics: Genomic sequencing, precision diagnostics
- Bioprinting: 3D tissue/organ printing
- CDMO: Contract development and manufacturing

**Investment screening framework (from webinar):**
1. **Industry Structure:** Antifragile or positive-forward rate of change; favourable 5 forces; APIs 2004-2019 lessons
2. **Business Economics:** Critical/essential product; high entry barriers; high exit barriers; low cyclicality
3. **Management:** Walking the talk; capital allocation track record; no major red flags; skin in the game

---

### 2.2 Genomics and Genomic Diagnostics

**Key concepts:**
- Genomic sequencing = analyzing exact order of individual's DNA (first done in 2003)
- 99.9% of human genes are identical — only 0.1% difference causes all diseases/variations
- Genomic diagnostics enables early detection of cancer and inherited diseases
- Cost of sequencing continues to drop dramatically — making it affordable for masses

**Investment opportunity:** Genomic diagnostics companies bridge expensive, uncertain diagnostic procedures with genetic clarity — allowing physicians to triage between biopsy (high-risk, expensive) vs CT surveillance (wait-and-watch).

**Case Study: Veracyte (global company, for benchmarking):**
- Genomic tests for thyroid cancer (Afirma), lung cancer (Percepta), breast cancer (Prosigna), lymphoma (LymphMark), ILD/IPF (Envisia)
- Total addressable market: ~$39B for pre-cancer detection; ~$10B currently addressed
- Partnership with Decipher Biosciences added prostate/bladder/renal cancer tests — now addressing 7 of top 10 cancers, ~1.1M patients/year globally
- $50B+ long-term global TAM

**Indian companies in genomics:** MapMyGenome.in (consumer genomics, "Know Yourself")

**Key genomics diagnostics risk:** Missing sequences — global genome database is only 2% African and 10% Asian ancestry. Indian population grossly underrepresented, limiting diagnostic accuracy.

---

### 2.3 CRISPR/Cas9 and Precision Medicine (Cell & Gene Therapy)

**What is CRISPR:**
- Gene editing tool — inserts, deletes, or replaces DNA sequences in a genome
- Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats + Cas9 (molecular scissors)
- gRNA guides Cas9 to the exact mutation; Cas9 cuts; cell repairs with healthy sequence
- Viral vectors are the current dominant delivery method

**Applications:**
1. **Sickle cell disease:** Victoria Gray was the first US patient cured via CRISPR (blood cells extracted ex vivo, edited, reinfused). 90k US patients, 40M worldwide.
2. **Blindness (LCA10):** EDIT-101 by Editas Medicine — in vivo CRISPR editing; CEP290 gene restored.
3. **Cancer immunotherapy:** CAR-T therapy — T cells extracted, modified with chimeric antigen receptor gene, multiplied, reinfused to destroy cancer cells.
4. **Organ shortage:** CRISPR edits pig genome to produce human-compatible organs (xenotransplantation via hiPSCs).

**CAR-T therapy economics:**
- Current cost: USD 1 million to 1.5 million per treatment
- Allogeneic (healthy-donor derived, off-the-shelf) dramatically cheaper and scalable vs autologous (patient-derived, one dose)
- Immuneel Therapeutics (India/Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw) aims to offer CAR-T at USD 50,000 — transformative if achieved

**Precision Medicine:**
- Lung cancer: ALK or EGFR gene mutations targeted by precision drugs without damaging normal cells
- 400M children worldwide suffer from inherited diseases — PM offers cure potential
- Reduces trial-and-error medication, avoids known side effects

**CRISPR risks:**
- Off-target edits elsewhere in genome
- Delivery challenge: Getting CRISPR only to target cells
- Immune response risk (Cas9 and AcrIIA4 are non-human)
- Regulatory pathway still evolving
- Ethical and biosecurity concerns

---

### 2.4 Bioprinting

**What it is:** Extension of 3D printing — using bioinks (cells + biomaterials) to print living tissues and organs.

**Potential:** Repair/replace tissues and organs using patient's own stem cells; mitigate organ donor shortage (>110,000 waiting in US).

**Current state:**
- Researchers (WFIRM — Wake Forest Institute) can bioprint ears, skin, muscle, bone for animal testing
- 4D bioprinting (Poietis + Servier): Liver model for drug toxicity testing
- Cellink (Sweden, listed on Nasdaq First North): Bioink manufacturer; 3,000 shareholders, 85% held by top 10 shareholders — thinly traded but worth tracking

**Challenges:**
- Every cell must be within 200 microns of blood supply — vascularization is the critical unsolved problem
- Complex tissue heterogeneity
- Regulatory uncertainty for bioprinted organs
- Immune rejection risk
- Bioprinting is also being used for cultured meat production (bio-inks + incubation → structured meat)

---

### 2.5 Agri-Biotechnology

- ~90% of major global crops (corn, soyabeans) are already genetically engineered (GMOs)
- CRISPR vs GMO: CRISPR does NOT introduce foreign DNA; only edits existing species genes — hence less regulated
- Japan: Approved gene-edited food without additional safety evaluations
- North America: Gene-edited crops without foreign DNA don't require GMO-level regulation
- EU: Heavily regulated like GMOs (France-led challenge)

**Applications:**
- Disease-resistant crops: CRISPR potato resistant to Panama disease fungus
- Drought-tolerant varieties
- Higher nutritional content (Vitamin C, B6)
- Reduced harmful chemicals (knocking out gene that converts starch to acrylamide during high-temp cooking)
- Biopesticides: Bioherbicides, biofungicides, bioinsecticides — USD 6.6B market (14% CAGR) vs conventional pesticides $85B

**Case Study: Tropic Biosciences (UK, unlisted):**
- GEiGS technology (Gene Editing induced Gene Silencing) on tropical crops (banana, rice, coffee)
- Panama disease resistance in bananas, extended shelf life (reducing ethylene), reduced-caffeine coffee
- BASF research agreement for crop protection (2020)
- Warning: "Gene editing process can take 10 years with no guarantee of success" — high-risk, long-dated payoff

**Indian Agri-biotech:**
- Very few Indian companies active in CRISPR gene-editing and biopesticides
- Rallis India (Tata) doing agri-biotech: GM traits for maize and cotton, designer microbes for soil nitrogen

---

### 2.6 Industrial Biotechnology: Enzymes, Fermentation, Precision Fermentation

- Enzymes used in: Food/dairy (cheese, yogurt), detergents, paper/textiles, biocatalysis for pharma
- Fermentation: Cultured meat, probiotics, nutraceuticals, ethanol, biofuels
- Precision fermentation: Producing exact proteins (e.g., casein for dairy alternatives) via engineered microbes
- CDMO plays a key role: CDMOs partner with gene-editing innovators to develop viral vectors for CRISPR delivery

---

### 2.7 How to Evaluate Biotech Companies (Investment Framework)

**Pipeline evaluation:**
1. How many molecules/therapies in clinical pipeline? Phase I, II, III?
2. What is the target indication — orphan disease (faster approval, less competition) vs mass market (harder to differentiate)?
3. Is the delivery mechanism proven (e.g., viral vectors, LNP) or experimental?
4. Total Addressable Market per indication

**Regulatory track record:**
- Number of USFDA/EMA approvals or IND clearances
- Any warning letters, import alerts, Form 483 observations?
- History of clinical trial failures in the company's therapeutic area

**Partnerships and licensing:**
- Licensing deals with large pharma = validation (e.g., Tropic + BASF, Veracyte + Decipher)
- CDMOs with long-term supply agreements with biotech = sticky revenue
- JV with global MNC = technology transfer + revenue certainty

**Key risks:**
1. **Clinical trial failures:** Phase III failure can destroy 80-90% of a biotech's valuation overnight
2. **USFDA issues:** Warning letters, import alerts, complete response letters delay revenue and signal quality concerns
3. **Competition from innovator companies:** Biosimilar market — innovators defend with secondary patents, REMS programs, authorized generics
4. **Manufacturing complexity:** Biologics require cold chain, clean rooms, specialized aseptic filling — far harder than generics
5. **CAR-T/gene therapy cost curve:** Market won't materialize unless cost comes down from $1M to $50-100K
6. **CRISPR off-target effects:** Clinical holds or safety signals can halt entire programs

---

### 2.8 Biosimilars — India's Core Biotech Strength

Though not the primary focus of the biotech webinar (which was future-oriented), biosimilars underlie India's biotech competitiveness:
- India has approved more biosimilars than any other country
- Key players: Biocon (in partnership with Mylan/Viatris for global markets), Dr. Reddy's, Intas, Hetero
- Biosimilar EBITDA margins: 30-40% for domestic market, higher for exports
- Regulatory risk: USFDA interchangeability designation is key for US market penetration

## PART 3: AROMA CHEMICALS

Source: SOIC Webinar text on Aroma Chemicals

### 3.1 What Are Aroma Chemicals?

Aroma chemicals are building blocks of fragrances and flavors used in:
- Perfumes, body mists, eau de toilette
- Soaps, shampoos, deodorants, body wash
- Floor/toilet cleaners, air fresheners, detergents
- Bakery, dairy, beverages, confectionery
- Pharma flavor masking (syrups, tablets)
- Agarbatti, camphor, essence oils

**Two types:**
- **Natural:** From plants, flowers, fruits, spices (lavender oil, lemon extract)
- **Synthetic:** Lab-made to mimic or create new scents (synthetic vanilla, synthetic rose)

---

### 3.2 Industry Structure and Value Chain

**Global Flavour, Fragrance & Aroma Molecules Industry:**
- Global fragrance and flavour industry TAM: Several tens of billions USD
- Growing at mid-single digits CAGR
- Highly consolidated at the top (global MNCs dominate formulation)
- India's F&F industry growing faster than global peers

**Value Chain:**
1. **Raw Material / Feedstock:** Turpentine oil (from pine resin/GTO — Gum Turpentine Oil), citrus oils, natural isolates
2. **Aroma Ingredient / Molecule Manufacturers (Ingredients):** Companies like PRIVI Speciality Chemicals — make camphor, terpineol, linalool, etc. (commodity-ish)
3. **Formulation Houses / Fragrance Houses (Formulators):** Companies like SH Kelkar, IFF, Givaudan, Symrise — blend molecules into proprietary fragrances used by FMCG companies
4. **FMCG / End User:** HUL, Marico, P&G, Godrej, Emami buy fragrances/flavours and embed in final consumer products

**Key insight on moat:**
- Formulators (SH Kelkar) are MORE moated than ingredients manufacturers because:
  - Fragrance formulas are trade secrets — switching costs very high
  - FMCG companies don't change fragrances easily (consumer recognition)
  - Relationships with R&D/marketing teams at FMCG are very sticky
  - Molecule manufacturers (PRIVI, Oriental Aromatics) are more commoditized — tracked by GTO/camphor prices

---

### 3.3 Key Companies

**SH Kelkar (Keva Fragrances):**
- Founded 1922, listed 2015; #1 Indian F&F company; top 10 globally in emerging markets
- Revenue breakdown: Fragrance ~85%, Flavours ~12%, Others ~3%

### 3.4 Pine-Based Aroma Chemicals

**Turpentine Oil (GTO):**
- Key raw material derived from pine resin
- Highly price-volatile — climate-sensitive (monsoon, drought affect pine yields)
- India imports GTO from China significantly — currency and trade policy risk
- Camphor is a major downstream derivative

**Camphor:**
- Derived from turpentine oil
- Applications: Agarbatti, religious use, Ayurvedic medicines, pharmaceuticals, insect repellent
- Oriental Aromatics and Mangalam Organics are primary camphor players in India

### 3.5 Competitive Dynamics

**Global MNCs (dominant, high market share):**
- Givaudan (Switzerland): Largest globally
- IFF (International Flavors & Fragrances, US): Post-DuPont Nutrition merger
- Firmenich (Switzerland): Merged with DSM into dsm-firmenich
- Symrise (Germany)
- All carry significant debt from M&A; slower growth than emerging market players

**Indian competitive advantage:**
- Lower cost base than MNCs
- Faster innovation for India-specific briefs (local flora, climate, cultural scent preferences)
- Proximity to large FMCG clients in India
- SH Kelkar growing faster than global peers at Indian FMCG price points

**Stickiness of F&F business:**
- Once a fragrance is embedded in a product and accepted by consumers, switching is extremely costly for FMCG clients (consumer recognition, regulatory re-approval)
- Long-term supply contracts being announced in the industry
- Very high customer retention → recurring revenue with pricing power

### 3.6 Investment Considerations (SOIC Conclusion)

1. **SH Kelkar:** Formulator with heavy FY25 investment in global CDCs. Near-term margin risk from Vashivali fire (recoverable via insurance). Opportunities from HUL contract and global expansion; guiding 16-18% margins from Q1FY26 onwards. Growing faster than international peers.

2. **PRIVI Speciality:** Ingredients leader; Givaudan JV adds strategic depth. Capex-heavy transition to bio-refinery carries execution risk. At 46x PE, growth optionality is priced in.

3. **Oriental Aromatics, Mangalam Organics, Kanchi Karpooram:** Pure commodity — track GTO and camphor prices only. No sustainable moat.

4. **Industry-wide risks:** Cyclicity due to RM (raw material) price fluctuations; fire risk (both PRIVI and SH Kelkar had fire-related losses); inventory day challenges; regulatory exposure (IFRA, FSSAI, USFDA, EU norms).

## PART 4: CROSS-CUTTING INVESTMENT PRINCIPLES (from all three webinars)

### Thinking about secular growth vs cyclical noise:
- Hospital sector: Structural underinvestment in India (12 beds/10,000 people vs global 29) provides secular tailwind. Short-term volatility from COVID, PMJAY pricing doesn't alter the multi-decade story.
- Biotech: Non-linear, multi-decadal. Single breakthroughs (CRISPR sickle cell cure, first gene-editing approval) catalyze step-change adoption.
- Aroma chemicals: India growing 12-15% CAGR due to FMCG and QSR expansion — secular but with commodity cyclicality in feedstocks.

### The moat hierarchy (all three sectors):
1. Formulator/brand (SH Kelkar in aromas; quaternary-care hospital with doctor brand in hospitals) — highest moat
2. CDMO/ingredients market leader with IP (PRIVI) — medium moat
3. Pure commodity (Oriental Aromatics, GTO-linked) — no moat

### Capital allocation red flags (applicable across sectors):
- High capex without ROCE improvement
- Geographic diversification before mastery of existing markets
- Acquisition-heavy growth without integration discipline
- Over-reliance on government business (hospitals) or government policy (agri-biotech)
