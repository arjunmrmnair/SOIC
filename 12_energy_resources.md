## Part A: Oil & Gas Industry — Structure and Fundamentals

### 1. Why Oil Matters

- Crude oil is the world's most important energy resource and transportation fuel; ~90% of all transportation fuels derive from crude oil
- Oil is the raw material for medicines, plastics, cleaning products, fertilizers, synthetic fibres, cosmetics, and thousands of everyday items
- "Hydrocarbon Society": in the 20th century oil supplemented by natural gas toppled coal as the dominant energy source; underpinned the great postwar suburbanization movement
- Three great themes underlying the story of oil: (i) oil as the engine of modern capitalism; (ii) oil as an instrument of national strategy and geopolitics; (iii) oil as the defining commodity of the "Hydrocarbon Society"
- Historical arc: John D. Rockefeller built Standard Oil on kerosene sales in the 19th century; gasoline was initially a useless by-product until the internal combustion engine created a new civilization
- OPEC formation (1960) and the 1973 oil crisis reshaped global geopolitics; oil powers loomed very large in the 1970s
- Shale revolution (fracking + horizontal drilling) from ~2005 onwards: the fastest and greatest addition to global energy supply in history — added 800% more energy to the US than wind and solar combined over the same period
- US oil production peaked ~1970, fell, then surged again post-2010 via fracking; natural gas production has soared even more dramatically

### 2. Oil Basics

**What is crude oil?**
- An organic liquid substance found below the Earth's surface, composed of molecules of different hydrogen and carbon atoms (hydrocarbons)
- Also contains impurities: oxygen, sulphur, nitrogen, heavy metal atoms
- Non-renewable; formed from decomposition of organic matter (plants, marine animals) during the Palaeozoic Era (245–544 million years ago) under enormous pressure and high temperatures

**Three conditions required for underground oil accumulation:**
1. A source rock rich in hydrocarbons, buried deep enough for heat to cook them into oil
2. A porous, permeable reservoir rock nearby (often sandstone) where oil can accumulate
3. A cap rock or seal to trap the oil; within reservoirs, hydrocarbons are layered: water below, oil in the middle, gas above ("three-layer cake")

**Crude oil properties:**
- **API gravity:** Measure of density. Higher API gravity = lower density = lighter oil. Determines distillate yields and which products can be separated
- **Sulphur content:** High sulphur = "sour" crude (0.5%+ by weight); low sulphur = "sweet" crude. Higher sulphur requires more treatment at refineries and is more corrosive and environmentally harmful
- **Light vs. Heavy:** Light crude = less metals and sulphur, flows easily, lower boiling range, higher value. Heavy crude = more carbon, less hydrogen, more time-consuming and expensive to refine
- **Reference benchmarks:** Brent Blend (North Sea), Dubai-Oman (Middle East), Tapis (Malaysia), West Texas Intermediate — WTI (USA)

### 3. Sector Structure: Upstream, Midstream, Downstream

**Upstream (Exploration & Production — E&P):**
- Activities: Exploration, drilling, and production of crude oil and natural gas
- Most risky and capital-intensive part of the energy sector; huge investments can be lost entirely, but large discoveries generate revenues for decades
- E&P company valuation driven by oil and gas reserves — the key untapped resources
- **Reserve Replacement Ratio (RRR):** Ratio of increase in reserves to annual production. RRR > 1.0 = growth in reserves; < 1.0 = reserve depletion. Target RRR ≥ 1.0
- **Finding & Development (F&D) costs:** Expressed in $/barrel of oil equivalent (BOE). Total costs incurred (property purchases + exploration + development) divided by annual proved reserve additions (BOE basis)
- **Operating costs (lifting/production costs):** Labour, energy, repair, maintenance, taxes, insurance, depreciation; vary widely by geography (deepwater vs. onshore)
- Cost of drilling an exploratory well: $1 million to $35 million; average time to discover, extract, and market oil = 3–10 years

**Midstream:**
- Activities: Transportation, storage, and wholesale marketing of crude or refined petroleum products
- Includes pipelines, rail, shipping, and storage facilities
- India: IOCL operates a 14,701 km network of crude, gas, and product pipelines (94.6 MMTPA oil capacity, 20.0 MMSCMD gas)

**Downstream:**
- Activities: Refining of crude oil, processing of natural gas, marketing and distribution of products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, lubricants, plastics, chemicals)
- Refineries perform three basic functions: Separation, Conversion, Treatment
- Cost to build a typical refinery: billions of dollars; employs hundreds of people, runs 24/7/365
- Highly automated and technologically advanced

**Integrated Oil and Gas Company:**
- Operates across multiple streams: drilling + refining + retail stations
- Examples: ExxonMobil, Chevron, Shell, BP, TotalEnergies, Saudi Aramco

### 4. The Oilfield Life Cycle — Investment and Value Creation

| Stage | Category | Key Activity | Risk | GCoS Range | Value Creation |
|-------|----------|--------------|------|------------|----------------|
| 1. Licence Award | Prospective Resources | Competitive bidding / direct negotiation | High geological | — | Concession agreement |
| 2. Explore | Prospective Resources | Geological/geophysical data, regional integration | Geological | 0–25% | Identification of GCos, defining prospective resources |
| 3. Discover | Contingent Resources | Drill exploration well, acquire logging data | Reduced geological | 25–55% | Commercial discovery of hydrocarbons |
| 4. Field Development | Reserves | Pre-FID planning; Post-FID: design, construction, installation | Low geological; oil price risk | 55–85% | Feasibility approval |
| 5. First Oil | Reserves | Commence oil production | Oil price risk | — | Beginning production |

| 8. Decommission | — | Abandonment | — | — | Environmentally safe closure |

**Key insight:** Highest value creation occurs in Stage 3 (Discovery) when new finds are made during exploration.

### 5. Extraction — How Oil is Produced

**Six steps to extract oil and gas:**
1. Acquire rights to explore and develop (reserve holder is land/block owner)
2. Conduct geological, geophysical, and seismic surveys
3. Perform exploration drilling to test for deposits
4. Conduct appraisal and development drilling (confirm commercial viability)
5. Begin oil and gas production (or abandon if no commercial deposits)
6. Pay royalties and/or production sharing agreements (PSAs)

**Drilling types:**
- **Land/Onshore:** Lower investment and fewer risks; crude extracted taken to gathering centres, water/sand removed, gas separated, oil pumped to storage tanks then to refinery
- **Offshore:** Used for oil deposits under the ocean floor; more than 65% of planned investments consumed before oil is even extracted — highest financial risk
  - Rig types: Land rig, Submersible (wetlands/swamps, up to 85 ft depth), Jack-up (shallow water, <400 ft), Semi-submersible (intermediate to deepwater, <10,000 ft), Drill ship (deepwater, <10,000 ft)
  - Land rigs can drill >30,000 ft; new drill ships/semi-subs can drill >30,000 ft in 10,000 ft of water
  - Workover rigs: Periodic maintenance and repairs; keep oil and gas flowing efficiently after wells are drilled

**American oil production and the shale revolution:**
- Two innovations enabled the unconventional revolution:
  1. Horizontal drilling: Steer drill bit sideways into long, thin formations — unlocked tight oil reservoirs not accessible with vertical drilling
  2. Hydraulic fracturing (fracking): Inject pressurized fluids to crack shale rock, creating pathways for natural gas to escape
- US shale expansion added 800% more energy than wind + solar combined over the same period (comparable in scale only to Saudi Arabia's Ghawar field opening 1965–1980)

### 6. Oil & Gas Drilling — Business Model and Cycles

**OGD (Oil & Gas Drilling) firms:**
- Own and operate rigs; customers are public/private E&P firms, IOCs, NOCs
- Main contract type: **Daywork contract** — driller leases rig to oil company at a specified day rate (dayrate) for a length of time
- Other contract types: Turnkey (lump sum after well completion), Footage (rate per foot drilled)
- Daywork = more favorable because it provides stable revenues regardless of whether oil is found

**Rig demand drivers:**
- Primarily driven by oil and natural gas prices
- High commodity prices → increase drilling at existing sites to maximize production → increase exploration for new supplies → rig demand rises
- Low commodity prices → reduce drilling activity, focus on acquisitions and developing existing reserves → rig demand falls

**World rig count dynamics:**
- Rig supply response: Build new rigs (bullish demand) or take rigs out of service (bearish demand)
- Build time: months for land rig, years for drill ship
- Rig utilization: Strong = high demand, rising dayrates, limited new supply; Weak = falling dayrates, rigs taken out of service
- Dayrates: Indicator of future rig supply; rising dayrates signal more rigs will be built

**Energy Equipment & Services (OFS) drivers:**

| Bullish | Bearish |
|---------|---------|
| Rising energy capex | Falling energy capex |
| Rising world rig count | Falling world rig count |
| Rising rig utilization | Falling rig utilization |
| Rising dayrates | Falling dayrates |

**Key questions when analyzing a drilling company:**
- Rig types: Land vs. offshore; shallow vs. deepwater; drilling for gas or oil?
- Contract type: Daywork, footage, or turnkey? Daywork preferred for revenue stability
- Dayrates: Current trend? Potential for expansion when contracts expire?
- Contract length: When do current contracts expire? Short or long term (can be beneficial or harmful depending on market conditions)
- Rig quality: Technologically advanced rigs command pricing premiums
- Rig location: Supply-demand dynamics vary considerably by region; mobile rigs have an advantage
- Rig fleet age: Expanding or retiring? Maintenance capex requirements?
- Operating and maintenance costs vs. peers: Control over cost inflation is critical for margins

**Capex cycles:**
- Upstream capex moves in cycles (reinvestment cycle → recovery cycle, repeated)
- In 2022, energy sector spent 64% less on capital expenditures than 2014 despite high oil prices
- HAL + SLB combined revenues -31%, PP&E -52.5%, capex -54.3% vs 2014 (2014 revenues $81.5B → 2023 $56.2B)
- Many fields need more drilling to offset aging field depletion — secular tailwind for OFS
- Late 2020s/early 2030s could see a reinvestment cycle

**O&G ROCE:**
- Long-term average ROCE for the industry: ~10%
- ROCE highly cyclical; peaked near 25% in early 2000s, went deeply negative in 2019-20, recovered to ~14.9% recently

### 7. Oil & Gas Equipment & Services — Key Services

- **Drilling equipment:** Drill bits, drilling fluids, mud pumps, drill pipes, wellhead equipment
- **Pressure pumping services:** Well cementing and stimulation; enhance production from wells
- **Wireline services:** Data recording using electronic instruments lowered into wells
- **Directional drilling and measurement:** Tools for directional drilling and data recording
- **Seismic imaging and analysis:** Sound wave-based detection of oil and gas deposits
- **Engineering and construction:** Design, build, operate oil and gas infrastructure — refineries, LNG plants, rigs, offshore platforms
- **Helicopters and boats:** Transportation to/from offshore rigs
- Global leaders: Schlumberger (SLB), Halliburton (HAL), Baker Hughes (BKR)

### 8. Downstream — Refining Concepts

**Refining process:**
- Crude oil is purified and treated to remove unusable substances (REFINING)
- Separates crude into different usable petroleum products
- All refineries perform three basic functions: Separation, Conversion, Treatment
- Products: Petrol/gasoline, diesel, jet fuel/ATF, LPG, naphtha, lubricants, bitumen, petrochemical feedstocks

**Gross Refining Margin (GRM) and Crack Spreads:**
- GRM = Revenue from refined products minus cost of crude oil input, expressed per barrel
- GRM is the primary profitability metric for refineries
- **Crack spread** = price of refined product minus price of crude oil (e.g., "3:2:1 crack spread" = 3 barrels crude → 2 barrels gasoline + 1 barrel distillate)
- GRM varies with: crude oil slate (light vs. heavy, sweet vs. sour), product slate, refinery complexity (Nelson Complexity Index), demand-supply for products
- Complex refineries that process heavier/sourer crude have structural advantage — cheaper crude input, higher value products = better margin
- Key variables for refinery companies: crude oil prices, refining margins (GRM), sales volumes, pipeline utilization, petrochemical prices

**India's refinery landscape:**
- 23 refineries: 18 public sector, 2 joint sector, 3 private sector; all upgraded to Euro VI standards
- Total installed capacity (March 2024): 217.11 MMT
  - IOCL: 28.15%; RIL: 24.05%; BPCL: 15.05%; HPCL: 8.47%
- IOCL is largest: controls 11 out of 22 Indian refineries, combined capacity ~80.7 MTPA
- Public sector crude throughput CAGR: 2.54% (FY07–FY23); Private: 6.66%

## Part B: Metals & Mining — Steel Industry

### 10. Carbon Steel Value Chain

**How carbon steel is made:**
Raw materials (Iron Ore + Coking Coal + Scrap) → Iron Making (Blast Furnace for BF route; DRI/EAF for sponge iron route) → Steel Making → Rolling (Hot Rolling → Cold Rolling → surface treatment) → Flat products or Long products

**Key profitability levers for steel companies:**
1. Backward integration in iron ore (captive mines = lower raw material costs)
2. Backward integration in coking coal fields
3. Higher value-added product mix in steel
4. Higher domestic vs. international subsidiary sales (India business generally more profitable)
5. Better cost structure vs. peers
6. Higher proportion of long vs. flat products (generally better margins in specialty long products)
7. EBITDA per tonne comparison across peers

**End user industries of steel:**
Automobile, Infrastructure, Real Estate, Construction, Railways, Consumer Durables, Capital Goods

**Chinese steel overcapacity:**
- China has massive installed steel capacity; when China exports more, Indian profitability dips
- Global steel cycle is heavily influenced by China's export volumes and domestic demand

**National Steel Policy of India:**
- Target: 300 MTPA steel capacity by 2030
- Over FY23-26: Indian steel industry adding ~30 MT of crude steel capacity

## Part C: Rare Earth Metals and India's Opportunity

### 14. What Are Rare Earth Metals (REMs)?

- REMs are a set of **17 metallic elements**: 15 lanthanides + Scandium (Sc) + Yttrium (Y)
- Found in diverse applications from energy storage to aerospace and defense
- Key applications: cellular telephones, computer hard drives, electric and hybrid vehicles, flat-screen monitors and TVs, wind turbine magnets, defense systems, MRI machines, catalysts, lasers

**The Rare Metal Wars context:**
- Geopolitical competition for control of rare earth supply chains has intensified (hence "rare metal wars")
- China dominates global rare earth processing (~85% of global refining capacity)
- Western nations and India racing to build alternative supply chains — strategic minerals now treated as national security assets

## Part D: Metals & Mining — Valuation and Investment Framework

### 18. Valuation Methods for Metals & Mining Companies

**Three primary valuation approaches used for cyclical metals/mining companies:**

**1. EV/EBITDA at Mid-Cycle Earnings:**
- Because metals are deeply cyclical, never value at peak-cycle EBITDA (will look cheap) or trough-cycle (will look expensive)
- Use **normalized / mid-cycle EBITDA** — average through-the-cycle earnings power
- Typical EV/EBITDA multiples for mid-cycle: 5–7x for commodity producers; lower for higher-cost, less-integrated producers
- Integrated producers (backward integrated into raw materials) deserve premium multiples vs. non-integrated
- International business drag on consolidated multiples: e.g., Tata Steel's international ops lower consolidated EBITDA/tonne significantly

**2. Replacement Cost / EV per Tonne of Capacity:**
- Asset-based approach: What would it cost to build this capacity from scratch today?
- Market should value a company's EV no lower than replacement cost, because if EV < replacement cost → no rational investor would build new capacity → supply constraint → cycle turns
- EV/tonne benchmarks vary by commodity and asset type:
  - Steel capacity: ~USD 500–700/tonne for a greenfield integrated plant
  - Aluminium smelter: significantly higher due to power intensity
- Useful for spotting companies trading well below replacement cost during troughs

**3. EV/Tonne (Production or Capacity):**
- Simple metric: Enterprise Value divided by annual tonnes produced or capacity
- Used for comparing same-commodity peers on relative basis
- Example from webinar: EBITDA/tonne analysis for steel companies (Tata India Rs.17,016; JSW Rs.12,110; JSPL Rs.12,522; SAIL Rs.5,623)
- For steel pipes, EBITDA/tonne varies widely by segment:
  - SS Pipes: Rs.14,000–16,000
  - DI Pipes: Rs.10,000–12,000
  - SAW + Carbon Seamless: Rs.6,000–9,000
  - ERW: Rs.3,000–5,000

### 19. Commodity Cycles — How to Think About Them

**Key cycle insight:**
- Commodity businesses are inherently cyclical: boom → investment → overcapacity → bust → underinvestment → shortage → boom
- Chinese steel capacity and export volumes are the most important variable for global steel cycle
- When China exports more steel, Indian profitability dips; when China domestic demand is strong, global prices rise

**Capital allocation is the most important differentiator:**
- Steel companies that allocate capital wisely (backward integrate, avoid dilutive equity issuances at trough, buy back at trough) create far more long-term value
- Companies with captive raw materials (iron ore, coal) have structurally lower and more stable cost curves
- Balance sheet strength matters enormously in cyclical industries — low D/E allows surviving troughs and investing counter-cyclically

**Ranking steel companies:**
1. Capital allocation quality
2. Cost structure: integrated vs. non-integrated
3. Expansion plans (at what cost? on or off the cost curve?)
4. Balance sheet strength (D/E ratio)

### 20. Industry Structure Internationally

**Global steel market:**
- Highly fragmented globally but consolidating through conglomerates
- Chinese overcapacity the dominant structural issue
- Indian majors expanding aggressively (Tata, JSW, JSPL) — structural demand tailwind from India's infrastructure and manufacturing buildout

**Capital allocation in conglomerates:**
- Conglomerates (Tata Group, JSW Group, Jindal Group, Aditya Birla Group) have portfolio advantages: cash flows from one business can fund another; brand equity; regulatory relationships
- Risk: Capital mis-allocation across too many segments can destroy value

## Part E: Natural Gas Economics

### 21. Natural Gas — Key Economics

**India natural gas sector:**
- India's natural gas share in energy mix is low (~6%) vs. global average (~24%) — large headroom for growth
- Government push to increase natural gas share to 15% by 2030 (City Gas Distribution, LNG imports, domestic exploration)
- Gas pricing: India has administered APM (Administered Price Mechanism) gas pricing for certain categories; other categories at market rates; key reform = market pricing for gas from difficult fields

**Key natural gas value chain participants:**
- **Upstream:** ONGC, Oil India (domestic production); imports via LNG (Petronet LNG)
- **Midstream transmission:** GAIL (62% of India's 27,516 km gas pipeline network); Gujarat State Petronet
- **City Gas Distribution (CGD):** IGL (Delhi), MGL (Mumbai), Gujarat Gas, IRM Energy, Mahanagar Gas, Torrent Gas — distribute piped natural gas (PNG) for households and CNG for vehicles

## Part F: Investment Checklists and Key Frameworks

### 22. Upstream E&P Companies — What to Look For

- **Total 2P/3P Reserves:** Breakdown in BOE (barrels of oil equivalent); compare proven vs. probable vs. possible
- **Onshore vs. Offshore Reserves:** Different risk profiles, cost structures
- **Reserve Replacement Ratio (RRR):** Must track >1.0 to be a sustainable E&P business
- **Production Costs ($/BOE):** Key competitiveness metric
- **Finding & Development Costs ($/BOE):** Total capital efficiency
- **Capex Plans:** Any planned expenditure for developing reserves — size, timing, funding
- **Production Sharing Contracts (PSCs):** Terms of royalty and profit-sharing arrangements with government/reserve holders
- **ONGC Key Variables:** Production volumes, wells drilled, exploration and reserve replacement, capex utilization, global oil/gas prices, government policies and regulatory environment, energy transition metrics, international ventures, technological innovation, market position

### 23. Drilling Companies — Analytical Framework

**Important questions to ask before investing:**
- Rig types and technology level (determines which commodity/region; technologically advanced rigs get pricing premiums)
- Contract types and breakdown (daywork = stable; footage = tied to drilling activity; turnkey = risky)
- Dayrate trend and potential for expansion on contract expiry
- Contract length (when do current contracts expire?)
- Rig location (regional supply-demand dynamics vary significantly)
- Rig utilization (growing, stable, or falling?)
- Rig fleet age and growth strategy (building new or retiring old rigs?)
- Operating and maintenance costs vs. peers

### 24. Steel Company Framework — Ranking Criteria

1. Capital allocation quality (avoid empire building; return cash when no good reinvestment opportunities)
2. Cost structure: Integrated (captive iron ore + coal) vs. non-integrated
3. Expansion plans: Cost, timing, strategic fit, on/off cost curve
4. Balance sheet strength: D/E ratio; ability to survive commodity troughs

### 25. Steel Pipes Sector — Key Risks Summary

1. Industry capacity addition outpacing demand — risk of margin compression
2. Current PE multiples (40–50x) unsustainably high; growth must materialize
3. Larger integrated players (Tata, Jindal) entering the tube/pipe space
4. Margins are inherently cyclical; exit discipline essential
5. Government-driven capex cycle dependency — political and policy risk
6. Short order book duration (6–18 months); limited revenue visibility

### 28. Key Commodity Cycle Principles (Cross-Sector)

1. Never value a commodity company at peak-cycle earnings — you will always pay too much
2. Never assume a commodity company at trough-cycle earnings is broken — if it has low cost structure and strong balance sheet, it will survive and reward patience
3. Track mid-cycle normalized earnings for true valuation anchor
4. Replacement cost sets the floor — when EV < replacement cost, industry self-corrects by stopping new investment
5. Chinese capacity and demand are the swing factor for most industrial commodities (steel, aluminium, copper)
6. For upstream oil: rig count, dayrates, and upstream capex are the leading indicators of the cycle
7. For refineries: GRM (crack spreads) vary with crude-product differentials and refinery complexity — complex refineries have structural moat
8. Integration into raw materials is the most durable competitive advantage in cyclical industries
9. Capital allocation in cyclicals: companies that buy back stock at cycle troughs and expand capacity at cycle bottoms create the most long-term value
